Generalized Disjunctive Programming (GDP) is a modeling framework for
discrete-continuous optimization that uses disjunctions and logic propositions as
first-class modeling constructs, rather than encoding all discrete decisions
directly as big-M constraints. The framework provides a structured intermediate
representation between a high-level problem description and a standard MINLP
formulation, making it possible to reason about reformulation strength and
algorithmic exploitation of problem structure.

** Foundations in Disjunctive Programming

The theoretical underpinnings of GDP trace back to Balas's work on disjunctive
programming citep:balas-1998. A disjunctive set is the union of finitely many
polyhedra, and the central result of disjunctive programming theory is a
characterization of the convex hull of such a set. Given a collection of
polyhedra $P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_k$, Balas showed how to construct the convex hull
of $P_1 \cup P_2 \cup \cdots \cup P_k$ using disaggregated variables and
additional linear constraints. This convex hull characterization is fundamental
because it establishes a tight LP relaxation for any optimization problem whose
feasible region can be expressed as a union of polyhedra. The key insight is that
the convex hull relaxation is provably at least as tight as any big-M
formulation of the same disjunctive set, and often strictly tighter. This
theoretical result underpins both the GDP reformulation hierarchy and the
strength analysis of MIP formulations for problems such as neural network
embedding (see [[file:neural-network-embedding.org][Neural Network Embedding]]).

** The GDP Framework

Raman and Grossmann introduced the logic-based modeling framework that became the
precursor to GDP citep:raman-grossmann-1994. Their approach replaces ad hoc big-M
constraints with explicit disjunctions and Boolean propositions as modeling
primitives. A GDP model consists of four components: continuous variables $x$,
Boolean variables $Y$, disjunctions that associate each Boolean variable with a
set of constraints (when $Y_i$ is true, the constraints in disjunct $i$ are
active), and logic propositions that express implications and exclusions among the
Boolean variables (for example, "if unit $A$ is selected then unit $B$ must also
be selected").

Grossmann and Trespalacios provided the definitive treatment of GDP as a
systematic framework for discrete-continuous optimization citep:grossmann-trespalacios-2013.
Their formulation takes the general form:

\begin{align}
\min \quad & f(x) \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & g(x) \leq 0 \\
& \bigvee_{j \in D_k} \left[ \begin{array}{c} Y_{jk} \\ r_{jk}(x) \leq 0 \end{array} \right], \quad k \in K \\
& \Omega(Y) = \text{True} \\
& x \in \mathbb{R}^n, \quad Y \in \{\text{True}, \text{False}\}^m
\end{align}

Here each disjunction $k$ contains terms $j$ that are activated by Boolean
variable $Y_{jk}$, and $\Omega(Y)$ collects the logic propositions. The key
advantage over direct MINLP formulation is that the disjunctive structure is
preserved, enabling the modeler and the solver to reason about which
reformulation strategy is most appropriate for each disjunction independently.

** Big-M vs. Convex Hull Reformulations

GDP models must ultimately be converted to MINLP (or MILP in the linear case)
for solution by standard solvers. Two principal reformulation strategies exist,
and the choice between them is one of the central decisions in GDP-based modeling
citep:grossmann-trespalacios-2013,grossmann-2002.

The big-M reformulation replaces each disjunction with algebraic constraints of
the form $r_{jk}(x) \leq M(1 - y_{jk})$, where $y_{jk}$ is the binary
relaxation of $Y_{jk}$ and $M$ is a sufficiently large constant. This produces a
compact formulation: no additional variables are introduced beyond the binaries.
However, the LP relaxation quality depends critically on the choice of $M$. Large
values of $M$ weaken the relaxation, leading to poor lower bounds and slow
convergence in branch-and-bound (see [[file:big-m-formulations.org][Big-M Formulations]]). Even with
tight $M$ values, the big-M relaxation is generally weaker than the convex hull
relaxation.

The convex hull reformulation, grounded in Balas's theory citep:balas-1998,
introduces disaggregated variables $x^{jk}$ for each term of each disjunction,
along with the constraints:

\begin{align}
x &= \sum_j x^{jk}, \quad x^{jk} = y_{jk} \bar{x}^{jk} \\
r_{jk}(\bar{x}^{jk}) &\leq 0, \quad \sum_j y_{jk} = 1
\end{align}

The LP relaxation of this formulation equals the convex hull of the disjunctive
set, providing the tightest possible relaxation obtainable from the disjunctive
structure alone. The price is a larger formulation: the number of continuous
variables and constraints grows with the number of disjuncts. Grossmann and
Trespalacios showed that the reformulation strength hierarchy is: convex hull
relaxation $\geq$ big-M relaxation, with equality only when the big-M values are
chosen to exactly reproduce the convex hull citep:grossmann-trespalacios-2013.

In practice, the choice between reformulations depends on the problem. For
problems with few disjuncts per disjunction and well-separated feasible regions,
the convex hull reformulation typically leads to faster overall solution times
despite the larger formulation. For problems with many disjuncts or where tight
$M$ values are readily available, the big-M reformulation may be preferable due
to its compactness.

** Logic-Based Solution Methods

Rather than converting GDP to MINLP and applying generic solvers, GDP models can
be solved directly using algorithms that exploit the disjunctive structure.
Grossmann's 2002 review places these methods in context alongside the four main
MINLP algorithms (branch and bound, outer approximation, generalized Benders
decomposition, and extended cutting plane) citep:grossmann-2002.

Logic-based outer approximation, developed by Turkay and Grossmann
citep:turkay-grossmann-1996, extends the classical OA algorithm (see
[[file:../methods/outer-approximation.org][Outer Approximation]]) to GDP. Instead of solving a single NLP relaxation
and generating linearizations for all constraints, logic-based OA solves NLP
subproblems for each active disjunction configuration and generates linearizations
only in the reduced space of the active disjuncts. This produces tighter
linearizations because inactive constraints (those whose disjuncts are off) do not
contribute spurious cuts. The method was demonstrated on process network synthesis
problems, where the disjunctions represent the selection of processing units, and
showed faster convergence than standard OA applied to the big-M reformulation.

Logic-based branch and bound operates similarly to NLP-based branch and bound
(see [[file:../methods/nonlinear-branch-and-bound.org][Nonlinear Branch and Bound]]) but branches on Boolean variables
rather than binary relaxations, and solves NLP relaxations that respect the
disjunctive structure at each node. By fixing Boolean variables rather than
binary values, the algorithm can prune infeasible disjunct combinations using
logic inference (constraint propagation on the Boolean propositions) before
solving any NLP, reducing the number of nodes explored.

** Connection to MINLP

GDP occupies a position between high-level problem formulation and low-level
MINLP representation. Every GDP can be reformulated as an MINLP (via big-M or
convex hull), but the reverse is not generally true: an arbitrary MINLP does not
necessarily have an obvious disjunctive interpretation. The value of GDP lies in
preserving the modeler's intent about the discrete structure, which enables
stronger reformulations and more efficient algorithms.

Grossmann's review citep:grossmann-2002 demonstrates that the hierarchy of MINLP
algorithms (OA dominates GBD, which dominates LP relaxation) carries over to the
GDP setting, with logic-based variants generally performing at least as well as
their MINLP counterparts. The MINLP survey literature
(see [[file:minlp-survey.org][MINLP Problem Class and Surveys]]) treats GDP as one of the
important modeling paradigms for structured discrete-continuous problems.

** Relationship to discopt

discopt's modeling API supports binary variables and linear indicator constraints,
which can express many GDP-style disjunctions via big-M formulations. The nn
module's ReLU big-M formulation is essentially a GDP reformulation where each
neuron's activation state is a disjunction: either the neuron is active
($y = \max(0, x) = x$, with $x \geq 0$) or inactive ($y = 0$, with $x \leq 0$),
formulated with big-M constraints on a binary indicator variable
(see [[file:neural-network-embedding.org][Neural Network Embedding]]). Balas's
convex hull theory citep:balas-1998 provides the theoretical basis for
understanding why tighter formulations (such as the Anderson et al. ideal
formulation) can dramatically improve solver performance on these problems.
