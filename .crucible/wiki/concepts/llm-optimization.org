** Introduction

Mathematical optimization is a cornerstone of engineering decision-making,
yet formulating and interpreting optimization models requires specialized
expertise that many practitioners lack. A chemical engineer may understand
their process intimately but struggle to translate operational constraints
into mixed-integer nonlinear programs. A supply chain manager may receive
a solver's infeasibility certificate without understanding which business
rules conflict. This expertise gap has long limited the adoption of
optimization tools outside specialist groups.

Large language models offer a potential bridge across this gap. Their
fluency in natural language, combined with emerging capabilities in
mathematical reasoning, positions them as intermediaries between
domain practitioners and optimization solvers. The vision is not for LLMs
to replace solvers (they cannot compete with decades of algorithmic
development in branch-and-bound or interior point methods), but rather to
serve as translators, diagnosticians, and assistants that make optimization
technology more accessible. As surveyed comprehensively by
citep:survey-optmodeling-llm-2025, this intersection of LLMs and
optimization modeling has seen rapid growth since 2023, spanning
autoformulation, diagnosis, explanation, and solver guidance. A broader
perspective on LLM mathematical reasoning capabilities and their
implications for optimization is provided by citep:forootani-2025-survey.

** Autoformulation: From Natural Language to Mathematical Models

The most active research direction is autoformulation: automatically
converting natural language problem descriptions into mathematical
optimization models. The NL4Opt competition established an early
benchmark for this task, challenging systems to extract optimization
formulations from textual descriptions of linear and mixed-integer linear
programs.

citet:ahmaditeshnizi-2024-optimus introduced OptiMUS, a modular LLM agent
that decomposes the autoformulation pipeline into distinct stages. The
system first parses the natural language description to identify decision
variables, objectives, and constraints. It then generates a mathematical
formulation, translates it into solver code (typically Gurobi or PuLP),
executes the solver, and evaluates the result. This modular decomposition
is crucial for handling long problem descriptions that exceed typical LLM
context windows, as each stage can focus on a manageable subset of the
information.

The accuracy of single-pass autoformulation remains limited, particularly
for problems with subtle constraint interactions or non-obvious variable
definitions. The MCTS-based autoformulation approach
citep:autoformulation-mcts-2025 addresses this by treating formulation as
a search problem rather than a generation problem. Each decision in the
formulation process (defining a variable, choosing an objective form,
specifying a constraint) becomes a node in a Monte-Carlo search tree. The
system can explore alternative formulations, backtrack from dead ends, and
evaluate partial formulations against consistency checks. This hierarchical
exploration significantly improves accuracy over greedy single-pass methods.

An alternative to prompting large proprietary models is fine-tuning smaller
open-source models for optimization-specific tasks. LM4OPT
citep:lm4opt-2024 demonstrated progressive fine-tuning of Llama-2-7b,
building capability incrementally from simpler to more complex formulation
tasks. The ORLM framework citep:orlm-2024 took this further with
systematic data synthesis and instruction tuning, showing that 7B-parameter
specialized models can outperform GPT-4 on benchmarks including NL4Opt,
MAMO, and IndustryOR. LLMOPT citep:llmopt-2024 proposed a five-element
universal formulation that standardizes the representation across linear,
integer, nonlinear, and combinatorial optimization, enabling a single
model to handle diverse problem types.

Despite these advances, significant gaps remain. Most autoformulation work
targets linear and mixed-integer linear programs. Nonlinear and
mixed-integer nonlinear programs (see [[file:minlp-survey.org][MINLP survey]])
present additional challenges: identifying convexity structure, choosing
appropriate relaxations (see [[file:convex-relaxations.org][convex relaxations]]),
and specifying nonlinear expressions correctly. The verification of
LLM-generated formulations remains an open problem, as subtle errors in
constraint specification can lead to models that solve without error but
produce meaningless results.

** Diagnosis and Explanation

Beyond formulation, LLMs show particular promise in helping practitioners
understand solver output and diagnose problematic models. This is the
focus of the OptiChat line of work from Can Li's group at Purdue.

The foundational work by citet:chen-li-2024-optichat-infeasibility
demonstrated that GPT-4, augmented with solver API calls, can effectively
diagnose infeasible optimization problems. The system extracts Irreducible
Infeasible Subsystems (IIS) from the solver and translates the mathematical
diagnosis into natural language explanations. An IIS is a minimal set of
constraints that, taken together, admit no feasible solution; removing any
single constraint from the set restores feasibility. While solvers can
compute IIS algorithmically, the raw output (a list of constraint indices
and mathematical expressions) is often opaque to practitioners. The LLM
bridges this gap by explaining which business rules or physical constraints
conflict and suggesting remediation strategies.

The full OptiChat system citep:chen-li-2025-optichat extends this to a
broader dialogue interface supporting model interpretation, sensitivity
analysis, and counterfactual explanations. Users can ask questions like
"What happens if I increase production capacity by 10%?" or "Why is this
constraint binding?" and receive grounded answers that reference the actual
model structure and solver output. The key architectural insight is that
the LLM alone cannot answer these questions reliably; it must be augmented
with tool calls to the solver that provide ground-truth numerical results.

The MOID framework citep:moid-2025 extends infeasibility diagnosis to the
multi-objective setting. Rather than identifying a single IIS and
explanation, MOID generates a Pareto front of diagnostic options that trade
off different remediation strategies. For routing problems, this might mean
presenting options that trade off relaxing vehicle capacity constraints
versus relaxing time window constraints. LLM agents then translate each
Pareto-optimal diagnostic into an actionable recommendation, giving
practitioners a menu of remediation choices rather than a single
prescription.

** LLMs as Optimization Solvers and Heuristic Designers

A natural question is whether LLMs can directly solve optimization
problems, bypassing traditional solvers entirely. The evidence to date is
largely negative for anything beyond toy instances. LLMs lack the numerical
precision, systematic search capabilities, and convergence guarantees that
decades of solver development have produced. They cannot reliably perform
the floating-point arithmetic required for interior point methods (see
[[file:ipopt-solver.org][Ipopt]]) or maintain the dual bounds needed for
branch-and-bound (see [[file:baron-solver.org][BARON]],
[[file:scip-solver.org][SCIP]]).

Where LLMs show more promise is in heuristic design and algorithm
configuration. They can suggest initial feasible solutions, propose
branching priorities, or recommend solver parameter settings based on
problem structure. These are tasks where approximate reasoning and pattern
matching (LLM strengths) complement the exact arithmetic and exhaustive
search of traditional solvers. The key principle is that LLM outputs should
inform but never override solver mathematics.

** Domain-Specific Applications

Chemical engineering has emerged as a particularly active domain for
LLM-optimization integration, driven by the complexity of process models
and the gap between process knowledge and mathematical programming
expertise.

citet:balhorn-schweidtmann-2024-flowsheet demonstrated LLM-based
autocorrection of chemical process flowsheets. By representing flowsheets
in SFILES (Simplified Flowsheet Input Line Entry System) notation, a
text-based encoding of process topology, the authors enabled transformer
models to process and correct flowsheet structures as sequences. The
system achieves approximately 80% top-1 accuracy in identifying and
fixing common flowsheet errors, demonstrating that LLMs can handle
structured engineering representations beyond pure natural language.
citet:schweidtmann-2024-genai-cheme provides a broader review of
generative AI across chemical engineering, situating optimization
applications within the larger landscape of molecular design, materials
discovery, and process synthesis.

More recent work on LLM-guided chemical process optimization
citep:llm-process-optimization-2025 employs a multi-agent framework where
specialized LLM agents handle constraint inference, model formulation, and
optimization guidance. A key capability is autonomous constraint inference:
the LLM agents identify implicit constraints from process descriptions and
engineering knowledge (thermodynamic feasibility, safety limits, equipment
ratings) that would typically require explicit specification by a domain
expert. This moves beyond pure formulation toward a collaborative
optimization workflow where the LLM contributes domain reasoning while the
solver contributes mathematical rigor.

** Connection to discopt

The discopt solver implements several ideas from this literature in its
optional =llm/= module (installed via =pip install discopt[llm]=). The
module uses litellm as a universal adapter supporting over 100 LLM
providers, with a safety invariant that LLM outputs never affect solver
mathematics.

Autoformulation is supported through =llm/tools.py=, which provides
OpenAI-format tool definitions and a =ModelBuilder= class for structured
=from_description()= calls. Users describe an optimization problem in
natural language, and the LLM generates a discopt =Model= object with
variables, constraints, and objective. The generated model passes through
=validate()= before any solver interaction.

The chat-based model building interface in =llm/chat.py= provides a
=ChatSession= for conversational, iterative model construction, similar
in spirit to OptiChat's dialogue approach. Users can refine their models
through multiple rounds of conversation, adding constraints, modifying
objectives, and exploring the solution interactively via =discopt.chat()=.

Diagnosis capabilities in =llm/diagnosis.py= mirror the infeasibility
diagnosis paradigm of citet:chen-li-2024-optichat-infeasibility. When a
model is infeasible, the diagnosis module analyzes the solver output and
provides natural language explanations of conflicting constraints. It also
handles convergence analysis for models that hit iteration or time limits,
and limit diagnosis for models that terminate prematurely.

The =llm/commentary.py= module provides a =SolveCommentator= for
streaming branch-and-bound commentary, translating the progress of the
solver (incumbent updates, bound improvements, node counts) into narrative
that helps users understand the solver's behavior. The =llm/reformulation.py=
module implements auto-reformulation detection, identifying common
modeling issues such as weak big-M values (see
[[file:big-m-formulations.org][big-M formulations]]), symmetry, bilinear
terms, and loose bounds, then suggesting specific improvements.

All LLM-generated content in discopt is sanitized through =llm/safety.py=,
which performs output validation, bounds clamping, and name sanitization.
This enforces the safety invariant: explanations and suggestions flow
from the LLM to the user, but formulations must pass validation, and LLM
outputs never modify solver internals, tolerances, or algorithmic
parameters directly.

** Open Challenges

Several fundamental challenges remain at the intersection of LLMs and
mathematical optimization.

Hallucination in constraint generation is perhaps the most critical
concern. An LLM may generate constraints that are syntactically valid and
even plausible-sounding but mathematically incorrect, either too tight
(rendering feasible problems infeasible) or too loose (admitting solutions
that violate the intended physics or business rules). Unlike hallucination
in text generation, where errors are often obvious, constraint
hallucination can produce models that solve successfully but yield
meaningless results. Verification of LLM-generated formulations remains
an open research problem; current approaches rely on test instances with
known optimal values, but this does not scale to novel problems.

Numerical precision presents a fundamental mismatch between LLM
capabilities and optimization requirements. Optimization solvers operate
with tolerances on the order of 1e-6 to 1e-12 (discopt uses abs=1e-6,
rel=1e-4, integrality=1e-5), while LLMs have no reliable mechanism for
exact arithmetic. This reinforces the design principle that LLMs should
assist with formulation and interpretation but not with numerical
computation.

Scalability to large models is another barrier. Industrial optimization
problems may have thousands of variables and constraints, with problem
descriptions spanning many pages of technical documentation. Current
autoformulation approaches struggle with this scale, as demonstrated by
the modular decomposition strategy in OptiMUS
citep:ahmaditeshnizi-2024-optimus. The MCTS approach
citep:autoformulation-mcts-2025 offers a promising direction by breaking
formulation into incremental decisions, but computational cost grows with
problem complexity.

Finally, the safety invariant (ensuring LLM outputs never affect solver
math) must be maintained as LLM integration deepens. As systems move from
explanation toward active participation in model building and
reformulation, the boundary between advisory output and solver-affecting
input becomes harder to enforce. Discopt's approach of requiring
=validate()= on all LLM-generated models and routing all LLM output
through safety sanitization provides a template, but the community will
need robust verification frameworks as these tools mature.
