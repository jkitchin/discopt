* Nonlinear Programming Fundamentals
:PROPERTIES:
:ARTICLE_TYPE: concept
:SOURCE_KEYS: nocedal-wright-2006
:STATUS: active
:CREATED: 2026-04-05
:END:

Nonlinear programming (NLP) is the foundation on which all continuous and
mixed-integer nonlinear optimization rests. The general NLP asks for a vector x
that minimizes a smooth objective f(x) subject to equality constraints c_E(x) = 0
and inequality constraints c_I(x) >= 0, where some or all of f, c_E, and c_I may
be nonlinear. The theory and algorithms for solving NLPs are developed in depth
by cite:nocedal-wright-2006, whose treatment provides the conceptual backbone
for the solvers used throughout discopt.

** Optimality Conditions and the KKT System

The first-order necessary conditions for a constrained optimum are the
Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions. At a local minimizer x*, there must exist
multiplier vectors lambda (for equalities) and mu (for inequalities) satisfying
four conditions: stationarity (the gradient of the Lagrangian vanishes), primal
feasibility (all constraints are satisfied), dual feasibility (inequality
multipliers are non-negative), and complementary slackness (each inequality
multiplier times its corresponding constraint value is zero)
citep:nocedal-wright-2006. The Lagrangian function L(x, lambda, mu) = f(x) -
lambda^T c_E(x) - mu^T c_I(x) collects the objective and constraints into a
single expression whose stationary points characterize constrained optima.

The KKT conditions are necessary only when a constraint qualification (CQ) holds
at the solution. The most common CQ is the Linear Independence Constraint
Qualification (LICQ), which requires that the gradients of the active constraints
are linearly independent at x*. A weaker but still useful condition is the
Mangasarian-Fromovitz Constraint Qualification (MFCQ). When no CQ holds, the
multipliers may not exist or may not be unique, and solvers can exhibit
pathological behavior such as cycling or failure to converge
citep:nocedal-wright-2006. In MINLP practice, degenerate constraint
qualifications arise frequently at nodes where fixing integer variables
introduces redundant or conflicting bound constraints, making CQ awareness
important for solver robustness.

Second-order sufficient conditions strengthen the first-order KKT conditions by
requiring that the Hessian of the Lagrangian is positive definite on the null
space of the active constraint Jacobian. When both first- and second-order
conditions hold, x* is a strict local minimizer. The distinction between
necessary and sufficient conditions is not merely theoretical: interior point
solvers exploit second-order information (via the Hessian of the Lagrangian) to
achieve fast local convergence, and the inertia of the KKT matrix directly
reflects whether the second-order conditions are satisfied
citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

** Newton's Method for Optimization

Newton's method is the algorithmic workhorse underlying virtually all modern NLP
solvers. For unconstrained minimization, Newton's method applies the iteration
x_{k+1} = x_k - H_k^{-1} g_k, where H_k is the Hessian of f at x_k and g_k is
the gradient. Each step minimizes a local quadratic model of the objective,
yielding quadratic convergence near a solution where the Hessian is positive
definite citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

For constrained problems, Newton's method is applied to the KKT system rather
than to the objective alone. The resulting iteration simultaneously updates
primal variables and dual multipliers by solving a linear system whose
coefficient matrix is the KKT matrix. This is sometimes called the
Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) approach when the Newton step is
interpreted as solving a quadratic subproblem with linearized constraints. The
primal-dual formulation used in interior point methods (see
[[file:../methods/interior-point-methods.org][Interior Point Methods]]) is
another manifestation of Newton's method applied to a perturbed KKT system
citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

The practical challenge with Newton's method is that the pure Newton step may not
decrease the objective or maintain feasibility far from the solution. Two
families of globalization strategies address this: line search methods and trust
region methods.

** Line Search Methods

A line search method computes a Newton (or Newton-like) direction p_k and then
selects a step length alpha_k > 0 along that direction. The step length is
chosen to satisfy conditions ensuring sufficient decrease in a merit function.
The Armijo condition requires that the merit function decrease by at least a
fraction of the directional derivative, while the Wolfe conditions add a
curvature condition ensuring the step is not too short. Backtracking line search
is the simplest implementation: start with alpha = 1 (the full Newton step) and
reduce alpha by a fixed factor until the Armijo condition is satisfied
citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

For constrained problems, the merit function must balance objective reduction
against constraint satisfaction. Classical approaches use an exact penalty
function (L1 penalty of constraint violation added to the objective), but this
requires selecting a penalty parameter. The filter line search used by
[[file:ipopt-solver.org][Ipopt]] citep:waechter-biegler-2006 avoids this issue
by maintaining a filter of non-dominated (objective, infeasibility) pairs, a
significant practical improvement described in the Ipopt article.

** Trust Region Methods

Trust region methods take a different approach to globalization. Instead of
computing a direction and then choosing a step length, a trust region method
defines a region around the current iterate (typically a ball of radius delta_k)
within which the quadratic model is trusted, and solves the subproblem of
minimizing the model subject to staying within the trust region. If the model
prediction agrees well with the actual function decrease, the trust region radius
is expanded; if the agreement is poor, the radius is contracted and the step may
be rejected citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

Trust region methods have stronger theoretical convergence properties than line
search methods in some settings, and they handle indefinite Hessians naturally
(the trust region constraint bounds the step even when the Hessian has negative
eigenvalues). However, the subproblem is more expensive to solve than a line
search step, and implementation is more complex. In the NLP solvers most commonly
used for MINLP (particularly Ipopt), line search methods dominate, though trust
region ideas appear in the SQP solvers sometimes used as alternatives
citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

** Quasi-Newton Methods

When second derivatives are unavailable or expensive to compute, quasi-Newton
methods approximate the Hessian using only gradient information accumulated over
previous iterations. The BFGS update is the most successful quasi-Newton formula:
it maintains a positive definite approximation to the Hessian and updates it at
each step using the secant equation. The limited-memory variant L-BFGS stores
only a small number of vector pairs rather than the full dense matrix, making it
practical for large-scale problems citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

In discopt, exact Hessians are available through JAX automatic differentiation,
so quasi-Newton approximations are not the default. However, when the Hessian
computation is prohibitively expensive (for instance, with very large neural
network embeddings), L-BFGS provides a viable fallback that trades convergence
speed for reduced per-iteration cost.

** Connection to Interior Point Methods

Interior point methods, described in detail in the
[[file:../methods/interior-point-methods.org][interior point methods article]],
are fundamentally an application of Newton's method to a parameterized KKT
system. The logarithmic barrier function converts inequality constraints into a
smooth penalty, and the resulting barrier KKT system is solved by Newton
iterations (the primal-dual step). As the barrier parameter decreases toward
zero, the barrier KKT system approaches the original KKT system, and the
solution of the barrier problem converges to the constrained optimum
citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

The line search and trust region globalization strategies discussed above apply
directly to the barrier subproblems. Ipopt uses a filter line search to
globalize the primal-dual Newton steps, while other solvers use penalty-based
merit functions. The convergence theory for these methods builds on the same
KKT foundations: the barrier parameter reduction schedule must be chosen so that
the iterates track the central path (the curve of barrier KKT points
parameterized by the barrier parameter) without jumping to regions where the
Newton model is inaccurate citep:nocedal-wright-2006.

** Relevance to Convexity and Global Optimization

The NLP theory in cite:nocedal-wright-2006 is fundamentally local: Newton's
method converges to a local minimizer, and the KKT conditions characterize local
optima. For convex problems, every local minimizer is a global minimizer, so
local NLP solvers suffice. The
[[file:convexity-detection.org][convexity detection]] machinery in discopt
determines whether this favorable situation holds.

When the problem is nonconvex, local NLP solvers can only find local optima, and
global optimization requires additional machinery: branch and bound to partition
the search space, convex relaxations to provide lower bounds, and bound
tightening to accelerate convergence. The NLP solvers at each branch-and-bound
node still use the Newton-based algorithms described here, but they solve
relaxed subproblems whose solutions bound (rather than equal) the global optimum.
