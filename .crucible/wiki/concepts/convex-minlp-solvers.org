Convex mixed-integer nonlinear programming (MINLP) occupies a distinctive
position in the optimization landscape: the problems are NP-hard due to
integrality requirements, yet the continuous relaxations are tractable convex
programs. This convexity structure enables a family of decomposition algorithms
that alternate between solving continuous NLP subproblems and discrete MILP
master problems, with rigorous convergence guarantees that do not extend to the
nonconvex case. The present article surveys the principal algorithmic approaches
and the solvers that implement them.

** Foundations: Kelley's Cutting Plane Method

The theoretical root of all polyhedral outer approximation methods for convex
MINLP is citep:kelley1960, which introduced the cutting plane method for
continuous convex programs. The idea is to approximate the convex feasible set by
an intersection of half-spaces (supporting hyperplanes), solve the resulting
linear program over this polyhedral outer approximation, and then refine the
approximation by adding a new supporting hyperplane at the LP solution point
where the most violated convex constraint is linearized. Because the feasible set
is convex, every supporting hyperplane is a valid inequality, and no feasible
point is cut off. The sequence of LP solutions converges finitely to the optimum
of the original convex program.

Kelley's method provides the conceptual template for
[[file:../methods/outer-approximation.org][outer approximation]] in the
mixed-integer setting: replace the LP relaxation with an MILP, and replace or
supplement the LP-based linearization strategy with NLP-derived gradient
information.

** Extended Cutting Plane (ECP)

citet:westerlund1995 extended Kelley's cutting plane idea directly to convex
MINLP. The extended cutting plane (ECP) method treats integer and continuous
variables simultaneously: at each iteration it solves a single MILP over the
accumulated linearizations, identifies the most violated nonlinear constraint at
the MILP solution, and adds a linearization of that constraint at the solution
point. No NLP subproblem is ever solved, which makes ECP particularly simple to
implement since it requires only an MILP solver.

The simplicity comes at a cost. Because linearizations are generated at MILP
solution points, which may lie far from the boundary of the nonlinear feasible
set, the cuts tend to be loose. ECP therefore typically requires more iterations
to converge than methods that generate cuts at NLP optima or on the constraint
boundary. Nevertheless, ECP became the algorithmic basis of the alpha-ECP solver
and one of the two core cut generation strategies in SHOT
citep:lundell2022.

** Extended Supporting Hyperplane (ESH) and SHOT

citet:kronqvist2016 introduced the extended supporting hyperplane (ESH) algorithm
to address the looseness of ECP cuts without incurring the expense of full NLP
subproblem solves. Given a point that violates a nonlinear constraint, ESH
projects it onto the constraint boundary and generates a supporting hyperplane
there. The resulting linearizations are tighter than those of ECP (because the
linearization point lies on the nonlinear boundary rather than at a potentially
distant MILP solution) while being cheaper than OA cuts (because a projection is
less expensive than solving a full NLP). ESH can therefore be understood as
interpolating between the cheap-but-loose cuts of ECP and the
expensive-but-tight cuts of classical OA. The algorithm retains finite
convergence for convex MINLPs.

The SHOT solver (Supporting Hyperplane Optimization Toolkit) citep:lundell2022
unifies ESH and ECP within a single-tree branch-and-bound framework. On the dual
(lower bounding) side, SHOT constructs a polyhedral outer approximation using
linearizations from both ESH and ECP, adding them as lazy constraints during the
MILP tree search via callbacks. On the primal (upper bounding) side, it employs
NLP solves at integer-feasible nodes and rounding heuristics. SHOT interfaces
with CPLEX, Gurobi, or Cbc as the underlying MILP engine and is accessible
through GAMS, AMPL, Pyomo, and JuMP.

Computational results in citep:lundell2022 show SHOT competitive with or faster
than Bonmin, DICOPT, and other dedicated convex MINLP solvers on standard
benchmark libraries. The single-tree design avoids the overhead of re-solving
MILP master problems from scratch at each iteration, which is a significant
practical advantage over multi-tree OA implementations.

** Bonmin: An Open-Source Algorithmic Framework

Bonmin (Basic Open-source Nonlinear Mixed INteger programming) citep:bonami2008
provides a modular framework for convex MINLP within the COIN-OR ecosystem. It
implements five algorithmic variants:

- B-BB: NLP-based branch and bound (see [[file:../methods/nonlinear-branch-and-bound.org][nonlinear branch and bound]]), solving the continuous NLP relaxation at each tree node using [[file:../methods/interior-point-methods.org][interior point methods]] via Ipopt.
- B-OA: Classical outer approximation following the Duran-Grossmann formulation, solving NLP subproblems at integer-feasible nodes and accumulating linearizations.
- B-QG: The LP/NLP-based branch and bound of Quesada and Grossmann, integrating OA into a single branch-and-bound tree.
- B-Hyb: A hybrid that uses B-BB as the tree search strategy while generating OA cuts opportunistically at integer-feasible nodes.
- B-ECP: Extended cutting plane within branch and bound, following citep:westerlund1995.

The computational study in citep:bonami2008 found that B-Hyb and B-OA tend to
perform best overall across a test collection of convex MINLP instances, while
B-BB is the most robust option for difficult problems where the MILP master
problem becomes expensive. Bonmin builds on Ipopt for NLP solves, Cbc for MILP
solves, and the CoinUtils infrastructure, making it fully open-source and widely
adopted in academic and industrial settings.

** Regularized and Quadratic Outer Approximation

A well-known weakness of standard OA is that the MILP master problem can propose
integer combinations far from the current incumbent, leading to NLP subproblems
that are difficult to solve or infeasible. citet:kronqvist2020 proposed two
enhancements that address this issue while retaining finite convergence
guarantees.

Regularized OA adds a trust-region-like penalty term to the MILP master problem
objective that discourages large steps in integer space. The penalty keeps the
next trial point close to the incumbent, producing more tractable NLP subproblems
and often reducing the total number of OA iterations.

Quadratic OA uses second-order information from the NLP subproblem, specifically
the Lagrangian Hessian, to construct a quadratic (rather than purely linear)
outer approximation. The resulting MIQP master problem provides a more faithful
local model of the nonlinear feasible set, leading to better integer predictions.
This approach connects outer approximation to
[[file:../methods/interior-point-methods.org][interior point methods]], which
routinely compute second-order information as part of the Newton step.

Computational experiments on 109 test problems in citep:kronqvist2020 showed
significant improvements from both techniques, with regularized OA being
particularly effective at reducing total solve time across the benchmark set.

** Solver Comparison and Practical Guidance

citet:kronqvist2019 conducted an extensive computational comparison of 16 solvers
for convex MINLP, including BARON, ANTIGONE, Bonmin (all five variants), Couenne,
DICOPT, KNITRO, SCIP, and SHOT. The benchmark used a collection of convex MINLP
instances and measured both solution quality and computational time. The study
analyzed how solver performance varies with problem characteristics such as
discrete density (the fraction of integer variables), degree of nonlinearity, and
problem size.

Key findings from the comparison include the following. BARON and SHOT emerged as
strong performers across the full test set. BARON showed particular strength on
problems with low discrete density, where the continuous relaxation provides
substantial information. NLP-based branch and bound (B-BB in Bonmin) proved most
robust on problems where the MILP master problem is expensive relative to the NLP
subproblems. The single-tree approaches (B-QG in Bonmin, SHOT) generally
outperformed multi-tree OA on problems requiring many iterations. The performance
profiles and statistical analysis in citep:kronqvist2019 provide practitioners
with concrete guidance for solver selection based on problem structure.

For the discopt project, the methodology in citep:kronqvist2019 is directly
relevant to the benchmarking framework in =discopt_benchmarks/=, which compares
discopt against BARON, Couenne, SCIP, and HiGHS using similar metrics and
performance profiles.

** Relationship to Other Methods

The convex MINLP solver landscape connects to several related algorithmic
families. [[file:../methods/outer-approximation.org][Outer approximation]]
provides the theoretical foundation for ECP, ESH, and the OA variants in Bonmin
and DICOPT. [[file:../methods/nonlinear-branch-and-bound.org][Nonlinear branch
and bound]] is the alternative paradigm that avoids MILP master problems entirely,
instead solving NLP relaxations at every tree node; Bonmin's B-BB mode implements
this approach. [[file:../methods/interior-point-methods.org][Interior point
methods]], particularly Ipopt, serve as the NLP engine in virtually all convex
MINLP solvers, providing both primal solutions and the gradient or Hessian
information needed for linearization.

For nonconvex MINLPs, the convexity assumptions underlying these algorithms
break down: linearizations at NLP solutions may cut off the global optimum. Global
solvers like BARON and Couenne use spatial branch and bound with convex
relaxations to handle nonconvexity, a fundamentally different approach from the
decomposition-based methods described here.
