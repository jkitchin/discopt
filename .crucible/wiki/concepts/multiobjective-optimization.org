A multiobjective optimization problem (MOP) has the form

  min  F(x) = (f_1(x), f_2(x), ..., f_k(x))    subject to  x in X,

with k >= 2 conflicting objectives. Unlike single-objective optimization,
there is in general no point that minimizes every f_i simultaneously;
improving one objective worsens at least one other. The solution concept
is therefore replaced: instead of a single optimum one seeks the set of
Pareto-optimal trade-offs citep:miettinen-1999 citep:ehrgott-2005.

** Dominance and Pareto Optimality

For objective vectors a, b in R^k, a /dominates/ b (written a ≺ b) if
a_i <= b_i for all i and a_j < b_j for some j. A feasible point x* is
/Pareto optimal/ (equivalently, /efficient/ or /nondominated/) if no
other feasible x yields F(x) ≺ F(x*). The image of the Pareto set in
objective space is the /Pareto front/.

Refinements: /weak Pareto optimality/ requires only strict improvement in
all objectives to dominate, and /proper Pareto optimality/
(Geoffrion, Kuhn-Tucker) excludes solutions with unbounded trade-off
ratios between objectives citep:miettinen-1999. In convex MOPs proper and
regular Pareto optimality coincide under mild regularity. In nonconvex
MOPs --- including almost all multi-objective MINLPs --- proper efficiency
is strictly stronger, and some scalarizations (notably the unweighted
weighted sum) generate only properly efficient points and therefore miss
parts of the nonconvex Pareto front citep:marler-arora-2004.

** Ideal, Nadir, and Utopian Points

Three reference points parameterize the objective space:

- /Ideal point/ z* with z*_i = min_{x in X} f_i(x), computed by solving k
  single-objective problems. It is almost never feasible in the MOP.
- /Nadir point/ z^N with z^N_i = max over the Pareto set of f_i(x). Unlike
  the ideal, the nadir is expensive to compute exactly for k >= 3, and
  standard payoff-table estimates can be loose or misleading
  citep:miettinen-1999.
- /Utopian point/ z** = z* - epsilon e, a strictly dominating shift used
  by reference-point and achievement scalarizing methods
  citep:wierzbicki-2000.

Ideal and nadir define the range (z^N - z*) used for objective
normalization, which is essential before applying any scalarization:
unscaled weights are meaningless when objectives have different units or
magnitudes citep:marler-arora-2004 citep:messac-2003.

** Classification of Solution Approaches

Multiobjective methods are traditionally classified by /when/ the decision
maker (DM) expresses preferences citep:miettinen-1999:

1. /No-preference methods/ --- return a single compromise point (e.g.,
   the point closest to the ideal in some norm).
2. /A priori methods/ --- DM specifies preferences (weights, goals,
   lexicographic order) before solving; a scalarized problem is solved
   once.
3. /A posteriori methods/ --- compute an approximation of the entire
   Pareto front, then let the DM select. Covered by [[file:../methods/scalarization-methods.org][scalarization]]
   sweeps, [[file:../methods/normal-boundary-intersection.org][normal boundary intersection]], and [[file:../methods/evolutionary-multiobjective.org][evolutionary]] methods.
4. /Interactive methods/ --- iterate between computation and preference
   elicitation, progressively narrowing the region of interest
   citep:miettinen-1999 citep:wierzbicki-2000 citep:miettinen-2008-pse.

A second, algorithmic, axis is /deterministic versus stochastic/:
scalarization with classical NLP/MILP solvers produces Pareto-optimal
points with optimality certificates; population-based evolutionary
algorithms sample the front stochastically and give no certificate, but
scale far better in k (number of objectives) and handle nonconvex,
nondifferentiable, black-box f_i citep:deb-2002 citep:emmerich-deutz-2018.

** Many-Objective Optimization

For k >= 4, commonly called /many-objective/ optimization, the fraction of
randomly sampled points that are mutually nondominated tends to 1, which
cripples dominance-based selection in evolutionary algorithms and makes
the Pareto front hard to visualize or present to a DM. Remedies include
decomposition into scalar subproblems citep:zhang-li-2007, reference-point
based selection citep:deb-jain-2014, and indicator-based selection
citep:zitzler-kunzli-2004. See [[file:../methods/evolutionary-multiobjective.org][evolutionary multiobjective]].

** Mixed-Integer and Nonconvex Structure

Combining multiple objectives with mixed-integer or nonconvex constraints
compounds the theoretical and computational difficulties. The Pareto set
of a multi-objective MILP is a finite but potentially exponential union of
faces of the LP relaxation polytope; for multi-objective MINLP it may be
disconnected, contain isolated points, and require spatial branching on
both x and a scalarization parameter citep:ehrgott-2011. Exact methods
and current practice are summarized in [[file:../methods/exact-mo-integer-programming.org][exact multiobjective integer
programming]].

** Context in discopt

discopt currently exposes only a single-objective interface through
~Model.minimize~ / ~Model.maximize~, so multi-objective problems are
handled today by user-level scalarization sweeps (weighted sum,
ε-constraint, weighted Tchebycheff) that repeatedly invoke the
single-objective solver. Adding ~ParetoFront~, ε-constraint automation,
and Chebyshev/NBI parameter sweeps is listed under Phase 5 of the project
roadmap.
