HiGHS is an open-source, MIT-licensed LP/MIP/QP solver developed primarily at
the University of Edinburgh. It pairs a modern parallel dual revised simplex
implementation citep:huangfu-hall-2018 with an interior-point method
citep:schork-gondzio-2020, a PDLP first-order LP solver, an active-set QP
solver, and a full branch-and-cut MIP engine with extensive presolve
citep:achterberg2020presolve. For discopt, HiGHS is both the default LP
backend called from Rust and the single most important /reference
implementation/ to study when deciding which MIP components to port, because
its permissive license makes source-level adaptation straightforward.

** License and Source Layout

HiGHS is distributed under the **MIT license** (verified in =LICENSE.txt= at
the repository root, =ERGO-Code/HiGHS=). This permits unlimited use,
modification, and redistribution, including line-by-line ports into Rust,
subject only to retention of the copyright notice. Algorithms themselves are
not copyrightable (US: Baker v. Selden, 17 USC §102(b)); only the specific
source expression is. The safe integration pattern for discopt is therefore:
re-implement from the papers where possible, cross-check against HiGHS
outputs on shared instances, and preserve the MIT notice + upstream filename
mapping in any module that is a close port. Two vendored sub-projects
(=highs/pdlp/cupdlp/= and =highs/ipm/basiclu/=) carry their own compatible
licenses that should be tracked separately.

The source tree concentrates logic in a small number of large files rather
than SCIP's plugin-per-reduction style. Key locations:

- =highs/simplex/HEkkDual*.cpp= — dual revised simplex with hypersparse
  FTRAN/BTRAN, dual steepest-edge pricing, and parallel CHUZR.
- =highs/simplex/HEkkPrimal.cpp= — primal revised simplex with Devex/steepest edge.
- =highs/ipm/IpxWrapper.cpp= + vendored =basiclu/= — Mehrotra predictor-corrector
  interior-point method with basis-preconditioned LU.
- =highs/pdlp/= — first-order primal-dual LP (cuPDLP-C) with optional CUDA kernels.
- =highs/qpsolver/= — active-set QP solver (Feldmeier, PhD thesis, Edinburgh 2022).
- =highs/presolve/HPresolve.cpp= — all LP/MIP presolve reductions in one file
  (see [[file:../methods/mip-presolve.org][MIP presolve]]).
- =highs/mip/HighsPrimalHeuristics.cpp= + =HighsFeasibilityJump.cpp= — MIP
  primal heuristics (see [[file:../methods/mip-primal-heuristics.org][MIP primal heuristics]]).
- =highs/mip/HighsSearch.cpp=, =HighsSeparation.cpp=, =HighsPseudocost.cpp=,
  =HighsCliqueTable.cpp=, =HighsImplications.cpp=, =HighsConflictPool.cpp=
  — branch-and-cut search, cut families, pseudocost branching, clique table,
  implication graph, conflict pool.

** The Parallel Dual Revised Simplex

The defining algorithmic contribution of HiGHS is the parallel dual revised
simplex of citet:huangfu-hall-2018. Classical dual simplex is sequential at
the level of CHUZR (choose-row) because the leaving variable depends on the
current basis. Huangfu and Hall observed that, given a good candidate pool,
multiple CHUZR trials can be carried out speculatively and then reconciled
through a limited-recourse rollback scheme, yielding real wall-clock speedups
on large LPs despite the inherent sequentiality of the Bartels-Golub update.
The implementation uses hypersparse FTRAN/BTRAN to exploit the sparsity of
right-hand-side vectors after update, dual steepest-edge pricing for
robustness, and a bundle mechanism that amortizes pricing work over multiple
iterations. The associated earlier paper citep:huangfu-hall-2018 introduced
the novel update techniques that make the parallel scheme numerically stable
in single precision.

The idiot crash of citet:galabova-hall-2020 — a quadratic-penalty warm-start
that solves a sequence of unconstrained convex programs — provides a fast
initial basis for dense real-world LPs and was the basis of HiGHS's strong
performance on the Mittelmann LP benchmark set. For discopt, the idiot crash
is a candidate for Rust reimplementation as a HiGHS-compatible warm-start
generator in =crates/discopt-core/src/lp/=.

** Interior Point and First-Order Methods

The HiGHS IPM uses a Mehrotra predictor-corrector with basis-preconditioned
LU factorization citep:schork-gondzio-2020. Crossover to a simplex basis
follows the Bixby-Saltzman scheme. The PDLP path (added in HiGHS 1.7, March
2024) wraps cuPDLP-C, a primal-dual hybrid gradient solver with optional CUDA
kernels; this is directly relevant to GitHub issue #37's proposal to
integrate MPAX citep:mpax-2024, since cuPDLP-C and MPAX target the same
first-order LP regime but use different host stacks (C/CUDA vs.
JAX). discopt's choice between them is architectural: MPAX plugs into the
JAX DAG and supports =vmap=/=grad= through LP solves; cuPDLP-C does not.

** MIP Branch-and-Cut

The MIP engine, written primarily by Leona Gottwald, follows the post-2000
canon: reliability pseudocost branching
citep:achterberg2020presolve,achterberg2009, best-estimate node selection,
and a full Chvátal-Gomory cut family (Gomory mixed-integer, mixed-integer
rounding, mod-k, implied-bound, clique, flow-cover) in
=HighsSeparation.cpp= and =HighsTableauSeparator.cpp=. The primal heuristic
portfolio includes the LP-free Feasibility Jump of
citet:luteberget-sartor-2023 (see [[file:../methods/feasibility-jump.org][feasibility jump]]), RENS, RINS,
feasibility pump, central rounding, randomized rounding, shifting, and
ZI-round citep:wallace-ziround-2010. Conflict analysis is integrated through
=HighsConflictPool=. Presolve shares infrastructure with LP presolve and
adds MIP-specific probing, coefficient tightening, and clique merging (see
[[file:../methods/mip-presolve.org][MIP presolve]]).

** Role in discopt

HiGHS is already discopt's default LP solver (=python/discopt/solvers/=);
issue #37 asks whether its primal heuristics and presolve reductions should
be ported into Rust alongside the existing FBBT/OBBT code in
=crates/discopt-core/src/presolve/=. The present literature review
(see [[file:../methods/mip-primal-heuristics.org][MIP primal heuristics]] and [[file:../methods/mip-presolve.org][MIP presolve]]) classifies each
component by suggested host language (Rust | Python | JAX | Warp) so the
porting effort can be prioritized. Three themes emerge: (1) tight per-node
heuristics (rounding, diving, shifting, shift-and-propagate) and all
presolve reductions belong in Rust; (2) coarse LNS heuristics (RINS, RENS,
feasibility pump) belong in Python, orchestrating recursive =Model.solve()=
calls; (3) Feasibility Jump is an outlier whose per-variable score loop
maps cleanly onto JAX =vmap= or NVIDIA Warp kernels, giving discopt a
GPU-batched feasibility engine that HiGHS's CPU-only implementation
structurally cannot match.

** Recent Activity

HiGHS releases in 2024-2025 added PDLP integration (1.7), GPU cuPDLP (1.10),
and a new HiPDLP IPM wrapper based on citet:schork-gondzio-2020 follow-up
work. The first HiGHS workshop was held at Edinburgh in June 2024 with a
second in June 2025. No single definitive HiGHS paper has yet been
published; citep:huangfu-hall-2018 remains the canonical citation, with
component papers cited individually for the IPM, idiot crash, QP solver,
and MIP engine.
