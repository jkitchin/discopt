* Performance Indicators for Pareto Front Approximations

Multiobjective algorithms return a finite approximation A = {F(x_1), ...,
F(x_n)} of the Pareto front. Comparing two approximations A, B or
reporting the quality of A requires a scalar /performance indicator/
I(A) or I(A, B). Unlike single-objective optimization, no single indicator
captures all desiderata --- convergence to the true front, spread along it,
and uniformity of distribution --- so benchmark studies typically report
several citep:zitzler-deb-thiele-2000 citep:emmerich-deutz-2018.

** Dominance-Preserving Indicators

An indicator I is /Pareto-compliant/ (or dominance-preserving) if
A weakly dominates B implies I(A) is at least as good as I(B). Only
compliant indicators can be trusted to rank approximations consistently
with the dominance relation citep:zitzler-kunzli-2004. The two most
widely used compliant indicators are the hypervolume and the additive
epsilon indicator.

*** Hypervolume (HV)

Given a reference point r that is dominated by every point in A,
HV(A; r) is the Lebesgue measure of the region dominated by A and
dominating r citep:zitzler-thiele-1999. Larger HV is better. HV is
strictly Pareto-compliant: if A strictly dominates B then HV(A) > HV(B).
It simultaneously rewards convergence, spread, and cardinality, which
makes it the de facto standard indicator in evolutionary multiobjective
benchmarks. Exact computation is #P-hard in k, with the best known
algorithms running in time O(n^{k/2} polylog n) for general k and O(n^2)
for k = 3; in practice k <= 5 is tractable and larger k requires
Monte-Carlo approximation citep:emmerich-deutz-2018.

Hypervolume is also a decision-theoretic attainment measure: it equals
the expected improvement of A over a uniformly-distributed goal in the
reference box, which motivates the [[file:../methods/bayesian-multiobjective.org][expected hypervolume improvement]]
acquisition function used in Bayesian multi-objective optimization
citep:knowles-2006.

*** Epsilon Indicator

The additive (multiplicative) epsilon indicator I_epsilon(A, B) is the
smallest epsilon such that every point in B is weakly dominated by some
point in A shifted by epsilon (multiplied by 1 + epsilon)
citep:zitzler-kunzli-2004. It is Pareto-compliant and cheap to compute
(O(k n^2)), but requires two sets --- a reference set, or a pairwise
comparison between algorithms.

** Distance-Based Indicators

These use a reference set P* (typically a dense approximation of the true
front or the union of all algorithms' outputs) and measure a distance
from A to P*.

- /Generational distance/ (GD): mean distance from points in A to the
  nearest point of P*. Measures convergence but not spread; not
  Pareto-compliant.
- /Inverted generational distance/ (IGD): mean distance from points in
  P* to the nearest point of A. Rewards both convergence and coverage;
  widely used in many-objective benchmarks alongside its Pareto-compliant
  variant IGD^+ citep:emmerich-deutz-2018.
- /Delta spacing/ and /spread/ metrics (Deb): measure uniformity of
  spacing along the front citep:deb-2002.

None of the pure distance indicators is Pareto-compliant in general;
when used alone they can reward a dominated approximation if it happens
to be uniformly spread.

** Cardinality and Coverage Indicators

- /Two-set coverage/ C(A, B): fraction of points in B weakly dominated
  by some point in A citep:zitzler-thiele-1999. Asymmetric; C(A, B) = 1
  and C(B, A) = 0 means A dominates B.
- /Ratio of nondominated solutions/: cardinality of A after filtering
  against a reference set.

** Choosing Indicators

Benchmarking practice distilled from citep:zitzler-deb-thiele-2000 and
citep:emmerich-deutz-2018: report hypervolume for the primary ranking,
IGD (or IGD^+) for convergence/coverage when a reference front is
available, and epsilon or coverage for pairwise comparisons. Always state
the reference point (for HV), the reference set (for IGD), and the
normalization used --- these choices can flip rankings between algorithms.

** Relevance for discopt

Exact scalarization methods in a MINLP solver can certify Pareto
optimality of each computed point, so the quality question reduces to
/coverage/ and /spread/ of the scalarization grid rather than
convergence. For populations of heuristic / surrogate-based solutions
(e.g., [[file:../methods/bayesian-multiobjective.org][Bayesian multiobjective]] or [[file:../methods/evolutionary-multiobjective.org][evolutionary]] wrappers around
discopt subproblems), the indicators above would be the natural quality
gate and should be part of any future multiobjective benchmark suite.
