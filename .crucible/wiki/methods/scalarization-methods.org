* Scalarization Methods for Multiobjective Optimization
:PROPERTIES:
:ARTICLE_TYPE: method
:SOURCE_KEYS: miettinen-1999 marler-arora-2004 haimes-1971 mavrotas-2009 steuer-choo-1983 wierzbicki-2000 ehrgott-2005
:STATUS: draft
:CREATED: 2026-04-24
:END:

A /scalarization/ replaces the vector objective F(x) = (f_1, ..., f_k) with
a parameterized scalar objective s_θ(F(x)) so that solving
min_x s_θ(F(x)) for varying θ produces Pareto-optimal points of the
original MOP. The art is choosing a family {s_θ} with three properties:
(i) every optimum of s_θ is Pareto optimal (/efficiency/), (ii) every
Pareto-optimal point is attainable for some θ (/completeness/), and
(iii) the parameter-to-front map is well-conditioned so that evenly
spaced θ give an evenly distributed front
citep:miettinen-1999 citep:marler-arora-2004 citep:ehrgott-2005.

Scalarization is the workhorse of deterministic multiobjective
optimization because each subproblem is a standard single-objective NLP,
MILP, or MINLP that certified solvers --- including [[file:../concepts/minlp-survey.org][MINLP solvers]] like
discopt --- can handle unchanged. The tradeoff is that sampling a
representative front requires tens to hundreds of subproblem solves.

** Weighted Sum

s_w(F(x)) = sum_i w_i f_i(x),    w >= 0,  sum w_i = 1.

The oldest and simplest scalarization. Every minimizer is weakly Pareto
optimal, and strictly Pareto optimal if w > 0 componentwise. For /convex/
MOPs (convex f_i, convex X), the weighted sum is /complete/: every
properly Pareto-optimal point is the minimizer of some w
citep:miettinen-1999.

Two fundamental limitations in the nonconvex case
citep:marler-arora-2004 citep:das-dennis-1998:

1. Points on a concave portion of the Pareto front cannot be recovered
   for any w >= 0 --- they are supported only by nonlinear envelopes.
2. Even on convex fronts, uniform weight spacing yields highly non-uniform
   front coverage: most of the weight simplex maps to a small portion of
   the front, so entire regions are missed unless the weight grid is very
   dense.

Objectives must be normalized before weights have meaning
citep:marler-arora-2004: a common choice is (f_i - z*_i)/(z^N_i - z*_i)
using the ideal/nadir range.

** Epsilon-Constraint Method

Pick one objective f_p to minimize, move the others into constraints:

  min  f_p(x)    s.t.  f_i(x) <= epsilon_i  for i ≠ p,   x in X.

Introduced by citet:haimes-1971 for bicriterion problems. Every optimum
is weakly Pareto optimal, and every Pareto-optimal point can be obtained
by an appropriate epsilon vector, with no convexity assumption required
citep:miettinen-1999 citep:ehrgott-2005. This is the key advantage over
weighted sum: /ε-constraint recovers the entire Pareto front, including
nonconvex and disconnected pieces/.

The price is subproblem feasibility management (the ε-bounds can make
subproblems infeasible), the need for a good grid of epsilon values
(ideal: spanning [z*_i, z^N_i] for each constrained objective), and the
distinction between weakly and strictly Pareto optimal solutions at
boundary constraints.

*** AUGMECON and AUGMECON2

citet:mavrotas-2009 addresses the weak-efficiency and efficiency-check
issues with /augmented ε-constraint/ (AUGMECON):

  min  f_p(x) - delta * sum_{i ≠ p} s_i / r_i
  s.t. f_i(x) + s_i = epsilon_i,    s_i >= 0,   x in X.

The slack variables s_i make every binding constraint contribute a small
linear penalty, guaranteeing strictly Pareto-optimal solutions, avoiding
redundant iterations, and supporting early termination via bypass
coefficients when a subproblem is infeasible. AUGMECON2 adds lexicographic
refinement. Both are widely implemented in GAMS/Python and serve as the
reference exact method for multiobjective MILP.

** Weighted Tchebycheff (Chebyshev)

s_{w,z*}(F(x)) = max_i w_i (f_i(x) - z*_i).

citet:steuer-choo-1983 established the weighted Tchebycheff (L_∞) method
as the theoretical complement of weighted sum: /every/ Pareto-optimal
point of a (not necessarily convex) MOP minimizes some weighted
Tchebycheff problem. It is thus /complete/ for nonconvex MOPs.

Drawbacks: the pointwise max is nondifferentiable (standard remedy: add
an auxiliary variable t with t >= w_i (f_i - z*_i), minimize t --- the
resulting problem has the same tractability class as the original when
objectives are linear or smooth), and minimizers can be weakly Pareto
optimal. The /augmented/ weighted Tchebycheff of citet:steuer-choo-1983,

  min t - rho * sum_i w_i (f_i - z*_i),

with small rho > 0, restores strict Pareto optimality in a way analogous
to AUGMECON.

** Achievement Scalarizing Functions

Wierzbicki's reference-point methodology citep:wierzbicki-2000 generalizes
the weighted Tchebycheff using a DM-supplied /reference point/ z^ref
(the goal) and an /achievement scalarizing function/

  s(F; z^ref, w) = max_i w_i (f_i(x) - z^ref_i) + rho sum_i w_i (f_i(x) - z^ref_i).

- If z^ref is unattainable, the optimum is the Pareto-optimal point
  closest to z^ref in the weighted L_∞ sense.
- If z^ref is attainable, the optimum strictly dominates z^ref in the
  weighted sum of objectives.

Achievement scalarization is the analytical core of interactive methods
including NIMBUS citep:miettinen-2008-pse and is naturally suited to
preference-guided search: the DM adjusts z^ref rather than numerical
weights.

** Lexicographic and Goal Programming

- /Lexicographic/: rank objectives, solve in order, each subsequent
  problem adds a constraint that higher-priority objectives stay at their
  optimum. Produces Pareto-optimal points only under uniqueness
  assumptions citep:miettinen-1999.
- /Goal programming/: minimize deviations (d^+, d^-) from DM-supplied
  goals, under weighted or lexicographic aggregation. Efficient for
  satisficing problems but does not automatically produce Pareto-optimal
  points unless augmented by efficiency restoration.

** Choosing a Scalarization

Synthesis of citep:miettinen-1999 and citep:marler-arora-2004 guidance:

| Method              | Convex MOP? | Nonconvex MOP?         | Parameter meaning |
|---------------------+-------------+------------------------+-------------------|
| Weighted sum        | Complete    | Misses concave pieces  | Trade-off rate    |
| ε-constraint        | Complete    | Complete               | Objective bounds  |
| Weighted Tchebycheff| Complete    | Complete               | Direction from z* |
| Achievement fn      | Complete    | Complete               | Reference point   |
| Lexicographic       | Partial     | Partial                | Priority order    |

For bicriterion convex problems, weighted sum is often sufficient. For
general MOPs --- and in particular multi-objective MILP/MINLP where
disconnected nonconvex fronts are the norm --- AUGMECON, weighted
Tchebycheff, or [[file:normal-boundary-intersection.org][normal-boundary intersection]] / normal-constraint
variants are the defaults.

** Relevance for discopt

discopt's single-objective ~Model.minimize~ interface composes directly
with any of these scalarizations: the user writes the scalarized
objective, optionally adds ε-constraints, and sweeps the parameter grid
using standard Python. A native ~ParetoFront~ API with ε-constraint /
Chebyshev sweeps and automatic ideal/nadir computation is planned for
Phase 5 of the roadmap.
