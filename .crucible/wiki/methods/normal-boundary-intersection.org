[[file:scalarization-methods.org][Classical scalarizations]] (weighted sum, ε-constraint, Tchebycheff) share
a common failure mode: even-spaced parameter grids produce highly
non-uniform Pareto-front samplings. Normal Boundary Intersection (NBI) and
the Normalized Normal Constraint (NNC) method are /geometric/
scalarizations designed specifically to produce even spacing in objective
space, irrespective of the front's curvature.

** Normal Boundary Intersection (NBI)

citet:das-dennis-1998 introduced NBI as a systematic generator for the
Pareto front of general nonlinear MOPs. The construction:

1. Compute individual optima x^{(i)}* = argmin f_i(x), giving /anchor
   points/ F(x^{(i)}*) in objective space.
2. The convex hull of anchor points (CHIM --- convex hull of individual
   minima) is a (k-1)-simplex. Sample points w on this simplex by convex
   combinations w_i >= 0, sum w_i = 1.
3. For each w, shoot a ray from the CHIM point Φ w inward along the
   quasi-normal direction -n (e.g., -Φ 1) and solve

     max_{x, t}  t     s.t.   Φ w + t n = F(x) - z*,  x in X.

4. Each subproblem's solution is a Pareto-optimal point; evenly spaced w
   on the simplex produce approximately evenly spaced points on the
   Pareto boundary.

Properties and caveats:

- Works for nonconvex MOPs and locates points on concave regions of the
  front that weighted sum misses.
- Does not guarantee Pareto optimality in general: for nonconvex fronts,
  the ray can intersect dominated parts of the boundary. citet:das-dennis-1998
  gives a dominance filter as a post-processing step.
- Requires pre-computed anchor points and normalization; ill-conditioned
  when anchors are nearly collinear.
- Natural fit for spatial branch-and-bound: each NBI subproblem is a
  standard NLP/MINLP, amenable to discopt-style solvers.

** Normalized Normal Constraint (NNC)

citet:messac-2003 proposed the Normalized Normal Constraint method as a
more robust geometric alternative:

1. Compute and /normalize/ anchor points into a unit range via the
   objective-space matrix L = diag(z^N - z*).
2. Generate evenly-spaced points X^{pj} on the utopia hyperplane (CHIM).
3. For each X^{pj}, solve

     min  f_k(x)     s.t.   N_i^T (F(x) - X^{pj}) <= 0  for i = 1, ..., k-1,

  where N_i are vectors from the k-th anchor to anchor i. The inequalities
  restrict the search to a reduced feasible region whose boundary is
  normal to the utopia hyperplane.

NNC fixes two NBI weaknesses: the explicit normalization step makes the
method scale-invariant, and the inequality (rather than equality)
constraints give well-behaved feasible regions. The /Pareto filter/ step
again removes any locally-dominated outputs.

NNC has become the standard geometric scalarization in engineering
design software and is widely implemented in Matlab/Python multiobjective
toolboxes citep:marler-arora-2004.

** Adaptive Weighted Sum

citet:kim-deweck-2005 introduced the /adaptive weighted sum/ (AWS) for
bi-objective problems. Rather than a fixed weight grid, AWS alternates
between weighted-sum subproblems and a front-refinement step:

1. Solve at the two anchor weights; estimate the front by a piecewise
   linear interpolation.
2. Identify the segment with the largest gap in objective space.
3. Introduce additional constraints (bounds in the gap region) and
   re-solve a weighted sum on the constrained problem.

AWS recovers concave portions of the front that pure weighted sum misses
(the added bounds play a similar geometric role as the normal-constraint
inequalities in NNC) and achieves near-uniform spacing without requiring
normalization. Extensions to k >= 3 objectives exist but are less clean.

** When to Use Geometric Scalarizations

| Goal                                     | Recommended method |
|------------------------------------------+--------------------|
| Uniform front coverage, smooth problems  | NBI or NNC         |
| Nonconvex front, engineering design      | NNC                |
| Bi-objective problems, minimal tooling   | Adaptive weighted sum |
| Disconnected / piecewise-nonconvex front | AUGMECON           |
|                                          |                    |

All three methods compose cleanly with discopt-class MINLP solvers: each
subproblem is a standard NLP or MINLP with extra linear constraints or a
single extra scalar variable, introducing no new solver requirements.

** Open Issues

- Anchor-point computation dominates cost when individual minima are
  expensive.
- Normalization depends on a good nadir estimate; in MINLP the nadir can
  be as hard as the MOP itself (see [[file:../concepts/multiobjective-optimization.org][multiobjective optimization]]).
- k >= 4 generalizations (e.g., many-objective NBI) lose the uniform-spacing
  guarantee because the CHIM simplex has exponentially many candidate
  directions; decomposition-based evolutionary methods like MOEA/D often
  scale better in high-dimensional objective space (see
  [[file:evolutionary-multiobjective.org][evolutionary multiobjective]]).
