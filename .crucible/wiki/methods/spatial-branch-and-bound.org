Spatial branch and bound is the dominant algorithm for deterministic global
optimization of nonconvex MINLPs. The foundational formulation of the method
for mixed-integer nonlinear problems was presented by
cite:smith-pantelides-1999, who combined a symbolic reformulation strategy
with systematic domain partitioning over both continuous and integer
variables. Earlier work on deterministic global optimization
citep:horst1996 had established branch-and-bound as a general framework,
but the Smith and Pantelides contribution was among the first to unify
factorable reformulation, convex relaxation construction, and spatial
branching into a single coherent algorithm for MINLPs.

** Spatial Branching versus Integer Branching

Traditional branch and bound for mixed-integer linear or convex programs
branches exclusively on integer variables: each node in the search tree
fixes or restricts the domain of a discrete variable, and the continuous
relaxation at each node is solved to provide bounds. For nonconvex
problems, however, this is insufficient because the continuous relaxation
itself may be nonconvex, so solving it does not yield a valid lower bound.

Spatial branching addresses this by also partitioning the domains of
continuous variables citep:smith-pantelides-1999. When a node's relaxation
is loose (the gap between relaxation value and incumbent is large), the
algorithm selects a continuous variable whose current domain contributes
most to the relaxation error and bisects (or otherwise partitions) that
domain. Each child node inherits tighter bounds on the branching variable,
which in turn produces a tighter convex relaxation. This interleaving of
integer branching and continuous-domain partitioning is what distinguishes
spatial B&B from classical MIP branch and bound. The variable selection
strategies that guide this process are discussed further in
[[file:branch-variable-selection.org][branch variable selection]].

** Symbolic Reformulation and Factorable Decomposition

A central contribution of cite:smith-pantelides-1999 is the symbolic
reformulation step that precedes relaxation construction. Given a general
nonconvex MINLP, the algorithm decomposes every nonlinear expression into a
sequence of elementary operations (addition, multiplication, division,
univariate functions) by introducing auxiliary variables. This is the
factorable decomposition originally proposed by McCormick
citep:mccormick1976, but Smith and Pantelides formalized it as a
preprocessing pass operating on the expression directed acyclic graph (DAG).

The reformulated problem contains only simple nonconvex terms (bilinear
products, univariate concave or convex functions, fractional terms), each of
which has a known convex envelope or tight convex relaxation. The expression
DAG representation is also the foundation for automatic differentiation and
interval arithmetic bound propagation in modern solvers. In discopt, this
decomposition is performed on the Rust-side expression IR before the JAX
relaxation compiler constructs the node relaxations.

** Convex Relaxation at Each Node

At every node of the search tree, the algorithm constructs and solves a
convex relaxation of the original problem restricted to the current
subdomain. Because the symbolic reformulation has reduced the problem to
elementary nonconvex operations, the relaxation is built term by term:
bilinear products are relaxed via
[[file:mccormick-relaxations.org][McCormick envelopes]]
citep:mccormick1976,smith-pantelides-1999, univariate nonconvex functions
are relaxed using secant lines and convex envelopes, and general twice-
differentiable terms can be handled by
[[file:alphabb-underestimators.org][alphaBB underestimators]]
citep:androulakis1995. The theory of
[[file:../concepts/convex-relaxations.org][convex relaxations]] for global
optimization provides the mathematical foundation for these constructions.

The optimal value of the convex relaxation at a node provides a valid lower
bound on the original problem over that subdomain. If this lower bound
exceeds the best known upper bound (the incumbent feasible solution), the
node is fathomed (pruned). If the relaxation solution happens to be feasible
for the original problem, it may update the incumbent. Otherwise, the node
is branched further citep:smith-pantelides-1999,tawarmalani2005.

** Integration with Bound Tightening

Smith and Pantelides recognized that the quality of convex relaxations
depends critically on the tightness of variable bounds at each node
citep:smith-pantelides-1999. Their algorithm incorporates range reduction
(bound tightening) as an integral component of the node processing
strategy, not merely as an optional preprocessing step.

[[file:bound-tightening.org][Bound tightening]] takes two principal forms
in modern spatial B&B implementations. Feasibility-based bound tightening
(FBBT) propagates constraints through the expression DAG using interval
arithmetic to contract variable domains without solving any optimization
subproblems citep:belotti2009. Optimality-based bound tightening (OBBT)
solves auxiliary LP or convex relaxations to determine the tightest possible
bounds on individual variables consistent with the relaxed feasible region
citep:ryoo1996. Both techniques can dramatically shrink the domain at a
node, which in turn tightens the McCormick envelopes and other relaxations,
often enabling aggressive pruning. In discopt, FBBT is implemented in the
Rust core via forward-backward propagation on the expression DAG, while
OBBT leverages the HiGHS LP solver.

** Convergence to the Global Optimum

Spatial B&B converges to an epsilon-globally optimal solution in finite time
for problems with bounded variable domains and continuous objective and
constraint functions, provided the convex relaxations satisfy a key
property: they must become exact (the relaxation gap must vanish) as the
subdomain shrinks to a point citep:smith-pantelides-1999,tawarmalani2005.
McCormick envelopes for bilinear terms satisfy this requirement because the
width of the relaxation is proportional to the product of the variable
ranges, which goes to zero under successive bisection. Similarly, alphaBB
underestimators converge to the original function as the domain diameter
shrinks citep:androulakis1995.

The convergence argument proceeds as follows. At each iteration, the
algorithm either prunes a node (tightening the global lower bound) or
branches (creating children with strictly smaller domains). Because the
domain is bounded and the relaxation error vanishes with domain diameter,
the global lower bound must eventually reach within epsilon of the true
optimal value. The finite-convergence guarantee requires that branching
rules ensure no variable domain remains unbisected indefinitely, a
condition satisfied by standard longest-edge or maximum-violation branching
strategies citep:horst1996.

** Implementation in discopt

discopt implements spatial B&B as its primary algorithm for nonconvex
MINLPs, following the general framework of cite:smith-pantelides-1999. The
Rust B&B tree (TreeManager) manages the search tree, node selection, and
bound management. Python orchestration in solver.py dispatches convex
relaxation construction and NLP subproblem solving to JAX (batched IPM,
McCormick relaxations, alphaBB underestimators) or to external NLP solvers
(Ipopt, ripopt). FBBT is implemented in the Rust core via expression DAG
propagation, and the factorable decomposition that Smith and Pantelides
formalized is performed on the Rust-side expression IR before the JAX
relaxation compiler builds node relaxations.

** Relationship to Other Methods

Spatial B&B is the nonconvex counterpart of
[[file:nonlinear-branch-and-bound.org][NLP-BB]] (which handles convex
MINLPs) and is the algorithmic framework used by competing solvers BARON
citep:tawarmalani2005, Couenne citep:belotti2009, and SCIP. It differs from
[[file:outer-approximation.org][outer approximation]] in that OA is a
decomposition method (alternating NLP/MILP subproblems) while spatial B&B
solves relaxations at every tree node. The key innovations of
cite:smith-pantelides-1999 relative to earlier global optimization
literature were the integration of symbolic reformulation with the B&B
tree, enabling automatic construction of relaxations for general factorable
MINLPs rather than requiring problem-specific relaxation derivations.
