Primal heuristics, as implemented in [[file:../concepts/scip-solver.org][SCIP]] and [[file:../concepts/highs-solver.org][HiGHS]], are algorithms
whose sole job is to produce feasible integer solutions for a MIP as early
and as often as possible during branch-and-cut. They do not affect correctness (an MIP solver is sound
without them) but they have an enormous effect on performance: an early
incumbent enables aggressive dual-bound pruning, and the quality of the
incumbent determines how tight the primal-dual gap closes before time limits
trigger. Modern MIP solvers run a portfolio of dozens of heuristics, each
specialized to a particular structural situation, coordinated by a scheduler
that learns which to call where citep:chmiela-learn-schedule-2021. This
article surveys the portfolio as implemented in SCIP and HiGHS, groups
heuristics by family, and records for each one a *suggested host language*
(Rust | Python | JAX | Warp) for reimplementation in discopt.

** The Host-Placement Rubric

discopt has four plausible implementation targets, each with a distinct
performance envelope:

- **Rust** — the right home for any heuristic that runs many times per
  B&B node, touches the LP basis or propagation queue, or is a tight loop
  over sparse integer arithmetic. Rust wins because it avoids Python call
  overhead, allows cache-friendly sparse data layouts, and sits in-process
  with discopt's existing FBBT/propagation code in
  =crates/discopt-core/src/presolve/=.
- **Python** — the right home for coarse-grained heuristics whose inner
  iteration solves a full sub-MIP or sub-NLP, because the per-iteration cost
  dwarfs any language-boundary overhead. All Large-Neighborhood-Search
  heuristics live here (see [[file:large-neighborhood-search.org][large neighborhood search]]), as does the
  [[file:feasibility-pump.org][feasibility pump]], Undercover citep:berthold-gleixner-undercover-2014,
  and sub-NLP.
- **JAX** — the right home for per-variable score updates that vectorize
  cleanly across a dense variable vector and benefit from =vmap= over
  restart replicas. The canonical case is [[file:feasibility-jump.org][feasibility jump]]
  citep:luteberget-sartor-2023, whose Lagrangian score update is a sparse
  matrix-vector product with a discrete argmin.
- **Warp** — the right home for heuristics expressed as *lots of small
  irregular problems in parallel* with scatter/atomics — e.g., running
  per-variable score updates across thousands of subproblem replicas, or
  propagating FBBT across an open B&B frontier. Warp's CPU backend is
  comparable to Rust; its differentiator is one kernel source that scales
  from CPU to GPU citep:nvidia-warp-2026.

** Rounding Heuristics

The simplest family: given a fractional LP solution $x^*$, round each
integer-constrained variable to a nearby integer value and check whether the
result is feasible.

- **Simple rounding** (=heur_simplerounding.c= in SCIP; part of HiGHS
  =shifting()=) rounds each fractional variable to the unique side where no
  row lock is violated, skipping any variable whose rounding would break a
  constraint. Reference: citet:berthold-thesis-2014 §4.1. **Host: Rust.**
- **Rounding with row-activity repair** (=heur_rounding.c=) iteratively
  rounds the most-fractional variable toward fewer locks, updates row
  activities, and repairs violations. Same reference. **Host: Rust.**
- **ZI-round** (=heur_zirounding.c=; =HPresolve::ziRound= in HiGHS) rounds
  each integer variable to the nearest integer that minimizes the
  "zero-integer" fractionality measure, using slack bounds to shift without
  violating rows. Reference: citet:wallace-ziround-2010. **Host: Rust.**
- **Shifting and intshifting** (=heur_shifting.c=, =heur_intshifting.c=)
  round and shift continuous variables to repair constraints. **Host: Rust.**
- **Octane** (=heur_octane.c=) searches vertices of the unit octahedron for
  0/1 LPs (Balas, Ceria, Dawande, Margot & Pataki, /Math. Programming/
  86:281-314, 2001). **Host: Rust.**
- **Randomized rounding** (=heur_randrounding.c=; HiGHS
  =randomizedRounding()=) rounds $x^*_j$ up with probability
  $x^*_j - \lfloor x^*_j \rfloor$, else down; repeats with different seeds.
  Reference: Raghavan & Thompson, /Combinatorica/ 7:365-374, 1987. **Host: Rust**
  on CPU; **Warp** for a GPU kernel that launches thousands of random-seed
  replicas in parallel, scoring by violation and returning the best.

All rounding heuristics are cheap (O(n) per call) but together they cover a
large fraction of easy MIPs — especially knapsack-like and
setcover-structured instances.

** Shift-and-Propagate

citet:berthold-hendel-2015 introduced shift-and-propagate as a pre-root
heuristic that lies between pure rounding and a full dive. It shifts
variables one at a time toward their rounded values, propagating domain
changes via the solver's FBBT engine after each shift. This allows the
heuristic to detect infeasibility early and repair violations that pure
rounding cannot. SCIP implements it in =heur_shiftandpropagate.c=;
HiGHS does not have a standalone shift-and-propagate module but the
functionality is folded into =shifting()= plus =HighsDomain= propagation.
**Host: Rust** — requires direct access to the propagation queue and
domain object, so it must live next to discopt's FBBT in
=crates/discopt-core/=.

** Diving Heuristics

A "dive" simulates a depth-first descent through the B&B tree: at each
level, select a fractional variable, fix it to one of its rounded values,
re-solve the LP from a warm-started basis, propagate domain changes, and
recurse. If the LP becomes infeasible or the dive hits a dead end, backtrack
one level and try the other rounding. All diving heuristics share this
machinery and differ only in the *score* used to pick the variable and the
direction to round. SCIP implements more than a dozen variants, all in
=heur_*diving.c=:

- **coefdiving** — min number of locks in the rounding direction.
- **fracdiving** — nearest-integer fractionality.
- **pscostdiving**, **objpscostdiving** — pseudocost-weighted scores.
- **guideddiving** — bias toward an existing incumbent.
- **veclendiving** — score based on objective coefficient change per
  constraint hit.
- **linesearchdiving**, **rootsoldiving**, **actconsdiving**,
  **intdiving**, **indicatordiving** — specialized variants.
- **farkasdiving**, **conflictdiving**, **distributiondiving** — score
  using infeasibility analysis or a Farkas-style dual argument
  citep:witzig-conflict-diving-2021.
- **nlpdiving** — each level solves an NLP relaxation (for convex MINLP).
- **adaptivediving** — multi-armed bandit over the above.

**Host: Rust** for all diving heuristics whose inner work is LP+propagation
(the majority). **Python** for nlpdiving, whose inner work is a full NLP
solve via the existing cyipopt wrapper. **Python** for the
adaptivediving bandit controller — the controller's compute is trivial
relative to its arms. Diving is essentially free to implement once the B&B
LP/propagation stack is already in place, because it reuses the same
machinery.

Key references: citet:berthold-thesis-2014 §4.3 for the family;
citet:witzig-conflict-diving-2021 for farkas/conflict variants;
Pryor & Chinneck, /Computers & OR/ 38:1190-1201, 2011 for distribution diving.

** Specialized and Feasibility Heuristics

- **Feasibility pump** (=heur_feaspump.c=; HiGHS =feasibilityPump()=)
  alternates LP projection and rounding. Already covered in
  [[file:feasibility-pump.org][feasibility pump]]. **Host: Python.**
- **Feasibility jump** (=heur_feasjump.c=, new in SCIP 9; HiGHS
  =HighsFeasibilityJump.cpp=) is an LP-free Lagrangian local search. See
  [[file:feasibility-jump.org][feasibility jump]] for detail. **Host: JAX** (batched restarts) or **Warp**
  (sparse scatter with atomics).
- **Undercover** (=heur_undercover.c=) fixes a minimum vertex cover of the
  nonlinearity graph, reducing an MINLP to an LP/MILP subproblem
  citep:berthold-gleixner-undercover-2014. **Host: Python**; the
  nonlinearity DAG it reasons over maps cleanly onto discopt's JAX DAG.
- **Sub-NLP** (=heur_subnlp.c=) rounds integers and solves the residual NLP
  with Ipopt. **Host: Python** (reuse cyipopt).
- **Multistart** (=heur_multistart.c=) random NLP starts. **Host: Python**
  driver with a **JAX** inner kernel for parallel start evaluation.
- **MPEC** (=heur_mpec.c=) Schewe & Schmidt, /Math. Programming
  Computation/ 11:95-118, 2019, DOI:10.1007/s12532-018-0141-x. **Host: Python.**
- **DKS** (=heur_dks.c=, new in SCIP 9) knapsack-style diving. **Host: Rust.**
- **Post-processing local search** — =heur_oneopt.c=, =heur_twoopt.c=,
  =heur_locks.c= — tight swap loops on the incumbent. **Host: Rust.**

** Port-Order Recommendation for discopt

Ranked by payoff-to-effort ratio:

1. **JAX prototypes** (high novelty, high leverage given discopt's stack):
   Feasibility Jump, Multistart inner kernel. These let discopt offer a
   GPU-batched feasibility engine that SCIP and HiGHS structurally cannot
   match.
2. **Pure Rust, no recursion** (each a ~200-line module): simple rounding,
   ZI-round, shifting, octane, trivial, zeroobj, oneopt, twoopt, locks,
   bound.
3. **Rust with propagation hooks**: shift-and-propagate, fixandinfer, all
   diving arms, DKS — gated on exposing the FBBT engine as a reusable
   Rust API.
4. **Python orchestrators** (see [[file:large-neighborhood-search.org][large-neighborhood search]]): RENS, RINS,
   DINS, crossover, mutation, local branching, proximity, feasibility pump,
   undercover, sub-NLP, multistart driver, MPEC, ALNS/scheduler.
5. **MINLP-specific where discopt wins**: Undercover (reuse JAX DAG),
   sub-NLP (reuse cyipopt), multistart (JAX =vmap=). These are where
   Rust+JAX architecturally beats C-SCIP.
