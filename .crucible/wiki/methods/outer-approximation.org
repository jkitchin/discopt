Outer Approximation (OA) is a decomposition algorithm for solving convex
mixed-integer nonlinear programs (MINLPs). The core idea is to alternate between
two subproblems: an NLP subproblem that optimizes the continuous variables for a
fixed integer assignment, and an MILP master problem that selects the next
integer combination by optimizing over a polyhedral outer approximation of the
nonlinear feasible set. OA was introduced by cite:duran-grossmann-1986 for
process synthesis problems in chemical engineering, and subsequently extended by
cite:fletcher1994 to handle nonlinearities in integer variables and infeasible
NLP subproblems.

** The Alternating NLP/MIP Sequence

The algorithm proceeds iteratively. Given an initial integer assignment, the NLP
subproblem is solved with all nonlinear constraints active and continuous
variables free but integer variables fixed. The NLP solution provides an upper
bound on the MINLP optimum and yields gradient information that is used to
construct linearizations (supporting hyperplanes) of the nonlinear constraints at
the solution point. These linearizations are accumulated into the MILP master
problem, which forms a linear outer approximation of the original MINLP. Solving
the MILP master problem yields a lower bound and a new integer assignment. The
process repeats, with the gap between upper and lower bounds closing
monotonically at each iteration.

For convex MINLPs (where the objective and constraints are convex in the
continuous variables), the linearizations are valid outer approximations: no
feasible point is cut off by any supporting hyperplane. This convexity property
guarantees that the MILP lower bound is valid and that the algorithm terminates
finitely at the global optimum cite:duran-grossmann-1986,fletcher1994.

** Convergence Properties

The finite convergence proof rests on two observations. First, because the set of
feasible integer assignments is finite, the algorithm can cycle through at most
finitely many distinct NLP subproblems. Second, the convexity of the nonlinear
functions ensures that each linearization provides a valid (non-cutting) outer
approximation, so the MILP master problem is a relaxation of the original MINLP
at every iteration. The MILP lower bound is therefore non-decreasing, and since
the upper bound is non-increasing, the gap closes to zero in finitely many steps
cite:duran-grossmann-1986.

When the NLP subproblem is infeasible for a given integer assignment, a
feasibility subproblem is solved instead, and the resulting linearization acts as
a "no-good" cut that excludes the current integer combination from future master
problems. This mechanism, formalized by cite:fletcher1994, prevents the algorithm
from revisiting infeasible assignments and is essential for finite termination.

** Relation to Benders Decomposition

The theoretical lineage of OA traces back to Kelley's cutting plane method for
continuous convex programs cite:kelley1960, which approximates the feasible set
with accumulated supporting hyperplanes and solves a sequence of LP relaxations.
OA extends this idea to the mixed-integer setting by using NLP solutions to
generate the linearizations.

Generalized Benders Decomposition (GBD) cite:geoffrion1972 is a closely related
decomposition that also alternates between NLP subproblems and an integer master
problem. The fundamental difference lies in what gets linearized: GBD generates
cuts from the dual of the NLP subproblem (linearizing the projected value
function), while OA linearizes individual nonlinear constraints. As shown by
citet:grossmann2002, OA produces a tighter master problem relaxation than GBD
because constraint-level linearizations carry more information than a single
Lagrangian dual cut. The dominance hierarchy is: OA master >= GBD master >= LP
relaxation of the MINLP.

** Extensions and Enhancements

*** Equality Relaxation and Augmented Penalty

The original OA algorithm requires convexity for its convergence guarantees. For
problems with nonlinear equality constraints or nonconvex functions,
citet:viswanathan1990 proposed two modifications. Equality relaxation (ER)
introduces slack variables in the linearizations of equality constraints, which
would otherwise make the MILP master infeasible. The augmented penalty (AP) adds
a penalty term for violations of linearizations from nonconvex constraints,
allowing the master problem to explore integer combinations that would be
excluded by strict linear cuts. These enhancements form the OA/ER/AP algorithm
implemented in the DICOPT solver.

*** Single-Tree (LP/NLP) Approach

Standard OA solves a full MILP master problem at each iteration, which can be
expensive. citet:quesada1992 proposed integrating OA into a single
branch-and-bound tree: instead of solving the MILP from scratch each iteration,
an LP relaxation with accumulated OA cuts is maintained across the tree. When an
integer-feasible node is found, the corresponding NLP is solved and new
linearizations are added as lazy constraints to all open nodes. This single-tree
approach avoids redundant MILP work and became the dominant implementation
strategy in modern solvers.

*** Extended Cutting Plane

The extended cutting plane (ECP) method cite:westerlund1995 simplifies OA by
eliminating the NLP subproblem entirely. At each iteration, ECP solves only the
MILP master problem and adds a linearization at the MILP solution point (at the
most violated nonlinear constraint). ECP requires only an MILP solver, making it
simpler to implement, but converges more slowly because linearizations at MILP
solutions (potentially far from the nonlinear boundary) are looser than those at
NLP optima.

*** Extended Supporting Hyperplane

The extended supporting hyperplane (ESH) algorithm cite:kronqvist2016 generates
linearizations at points on the boundary of the nonlinear feasible set by
projecting the current MILP solution onto the nearest constraint surface. ESH
produces tighter cuts than ECP (closer to the nonlinear boundary) while avoiding
the full cost of an NLP solve. ESH can be viewed as interpolating between ECP
(cheap, loose cuts) and OA (expensive, tight cuts).

*** Regularization and Second-Order Information

A weakness of standard OA is that the MILP master problem can propose integer
combinations far from the current best solution, leading to difficult NLP
subproblems. citet:kronqvist2020 address this with two techniques: regularized OA
adds a trust-region penalty to keep integer steps small, and quadratic OA uses
second-order information (the Lagrangian Hessian) to build MIQP master problems
that better approximate the nonlinear landscape. Both retain finite convergence
guarantees and reduce total solve time on benchmark collections.

** Modern Implementations

*** DICOPT

DICOPT (DIscrete and Continuous OPTimizer), developed by Viswanathan and
Grossmann, implements OA/ER/AP and is available through GAMS. It was one of the
first practical MINLP solvers and remains widely used for process systems
engineering problems cite:viswanathan1990.

*** Bonmin

Bonmin (Basic Open-source Nonlinear Mixed INteger programming) cite:bonami2008
implements several OA variants within the COIN-OR framework: standard OA (B-OA),
LP/NLP branch and bound (B-QG), NLP-based branch and bound (B-BB), a hybrid
combining B-BB with OA cuts (B-Hyb), and ECP within branch and bound (B-ECP).
Bonmin builds on Ipopt (NLP), Cbc (MILP), and CoinUtils. Computational
comparisons show B-Hyb and B-OA perform best overall for convex MINLPs.

*** SHOT

SHOT (Supporting Hyperplane Optimization Toolkit) cite:lundell2022 combines ESH
and ECP linearization strategies with primal heuristics in a single-tree
framework. SHOT uses lazy constraint callbacks to add linearizations during the
MILP tree search, avoiding repeated MILP re-solves. It interfaces with CPLEX,
Gurobi, or Cbc as the underlying MILP solver and is available through GAMS,
AMPL, Pyomo, and JuMP. Benchmarks show SHOT is competitive with or faster than
Bonmin and DICOPT on many convex MINLP instances.

** Relationship to Other Methods

OA is one of four main algorithmic families for convex MINLP, alongside
branch-and-bound, GBD, and ECP cite:grossmann2002. Compared to
[[file:nonlinear-branch-and-bound.org][NLP-based branch and bound (NLP-BB)]], OA
adds linearization cuts that reduce the number of tree nodes explored, at the
cost of managing the polyhedral approximation and solving MILP master problems.
NLP-BB is simpler (no MILP solver needed) and benefits from batched GPU solving,
while OA typically explores fewer nodes due to tighter relaxations. The choice
depends on the relative cost of NLP versus MILP solves and the tightness of the
continuous relaxation.

For nonconvex MINLPs, OA linearizations are not valid outer approximations
because they may cut off the global optimum.
[[file:spatial-branch-and-bound.org][Spatial branch and bound]] with
[[file:../concepts/convex-relaxations.org][convex relaxations]] (such as
[[file:mccormick-relaxations.org][McCormick envelopes]] or
[[file:alphabb-underestimators.org][alphaBB underestimators]]) is required for
global optimality guarantees in the nonconvex case. The OA/ER/AP approach in
DICOPT provides a practical heuristic for mildly nonconvex problems by penalizing
rather than enforcing the invalid cuts.
