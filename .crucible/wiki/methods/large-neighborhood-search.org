Large Neighborhood Search (LNS), as implemented in [[file:../concepts/scip-solver.org][SCIP]] and [[file:../concepts/highs-solver.org][HiGHS]], is
the family of primal heuristics that construct a /restricted sub-MIP/ by fixing or constraining a subset of the
original variables, then call the MIP solver recursively on the restricted
model with a small resource budget (node limit, time limit, stall limit). If
the sub-MIP finds an improving solution, it becomes the new incumbent;
otherwise the heuristic returns empty-handed and the tree search continues.
LNS heuristics are the workhorses of modern MIP — on the MIPLIB benchmark,
solutions found by LNS dominate the final incumbent pool on most instances.
They share a common cost structure (one sub-MIP solve per call) and differ
only in the /rule/ used to pick which variables to fix.

** Why LNS Belongs in Python for discopt

Every LNS heuristic's inner iteration is a full sub-MIP solve — typically
tens of milliseconds to many seconds of compute. Whatever happens in the
surrounding orchestration — building the restricted model, adding a distance
constraint, invoking the recursive =Model.solve()= — is microseconds of
Python overhead at most. The rubric is unambiguous: **every LNS heuristic
is Suggested host: Python**. What matters for porting is exposing a
=Model.clone()= + =Model.fix_variable()= + =Model.add_distance_constraint()=
API in discopt's modeling layer, so that each LNS variant becomes a ~100-
line Python module that constructs a restricted model and recurses. The
heavy lifting — LP solving, branching, propagation — happens in Rust on
the way down, not in the Python orchestrator.

The one caveat is the /controller/ heuristic (ALNS or the newer Scheduler)
that decides /which/ LNS variant to call and with /what/ parameters. Its
compute is a bandit update or a learned scheduler — trivial relative to the
arms it invokes. It, too, belongs in Python.

** The Canonical LNS Family

*** RINS — Relaxation Induced Neighborhood Search

citet:danna2005rins observed that when the LP relaxation and the current
incumbent /agree/ on a subset of integer variables, those variables are
likely to be correct in the optimal solution as well. RINS fixes exactly
those agreeing variables and solves a sub-MIP on the rest. SCIP:
=heur_rins.c=. HiGHS: =HighsPrimalHeuristics::RINS()=. RINS is one of the
single most effective LNS heuristics in practice and appears first in most
benchmarks.

*** RENS — Relaxation Enforced Neighborhood Search

citet:berthold-rens-2014 noticed that when a variable's LP value is already
integer-valued, it does not need to be rounded — it can simply be fixed.
RENS fixes all integer-valued LP variables and bounds the fractional ones to
the two rounding neighbors, yielding a small sub-MIP that represents the
/optimal rounding/ of the relaxation. SCIP: =heur_rens.c=. HiGHS:
=HighsPrimalHeuristics::RENS()=. On relaxation solutions with many
already-integer values (typical for set-covering and fixed-charge network
flow), RENS finds a feasible incumbent almost immediately.

*** DINS — Distance-Induced Neighborhood Search

citet:ghosh2007dins combined RINS with a local-branching-style distance
constraint: fix a subset of variables agreeing between LP and incumbent, and
additionally require that the Hamming distance from the incumbent stays
below a threshold. SCIP: =heur_dins.c=. The distance constraint is a
single linear inequality, so the sub-MIP remains compact.

*** Local branching

citet:fischetti-lodi-2003 introduced local branching as a generic
neighborhood operator: given incumbent $\bar{x}$, add the constraint
$\Delta(x, \bar{x}) \le k$ where $\Delta$ is a Hamming-like distance on the
0-1 variables, then solve the restricted MIP. Branching on this distance
inside the recursive solve yields a "right branch" (close to $\bar{x}$) and
a "left branch" (at least $k+1$ away) which can be searched alternately.
SCIP: =heur_localbranching.c=. This is the earliest LNS heuristic and its
distance-constraint mechanism is the template for DINS and proximity search.

*** Crossover and Mutation

citet:rothberg-crossover-2007 adapted evolutionary operators to the MIP
setting. Given a pool of incumbent solutions, *crossover* fixes variables
that agree across two or more solutions and solves a sub-MIP on the
disagreeing ones; *mutation* randomly fixes a subset of integers in the
incumbent and re-optimizes the rest. SCIP: =heur_crossover.c=,
=heur_mutation.c=. These are the heuristics that power SCIP's solution
polishing phase once an incumbent is available.

*** Proximity Search

citet:fischetti-monaci-proximity-2014 proposed replacing the original
objective with a distance-to-incumbent objective, subject to the constraint
that the true objective improves by at least a small amount. The resulting
sub-MIP searches for solutions that are both improving and close to the
current incumbent — often finding improvements that local branching
(which rejects equal-quality solutions) would miss. SCIP:
=heur_proximity.c=.

*** GINS — Graph-Induced Neighborhood Search

GINS (Maher et al., in Berthold & Hendel) selects a connected component in
the variable-constraint bipartite graph around a seed variable and fixes
everything outside that component. The fix is structural rather than
relaxation-induced, which makes GINS useful on problems where the LP gives
little guidance. SCIP: =heur_gins.c=.

*** Other LNS Variants

- **Trust region** (=heur_trustregion.c=) — Maher, for Benders decomposition
  (SCIP 9 release paper, citet:bolusani-scip9-2024).
- **LP face** (=heur_lpface.c=) — fixes variables active on the optimal LP
  face.
- **Completesol** (=heur_completesol.c=) — given a partial user solution,
  solve a sub-MIP for the rest.
- **OFINS** (=heur_ofins.c=) — objective-function-induced neighborhood
  search, used in reoptimization.

** ALNS and the SCIP Scheduler

citet:chmiela-learn-schedule-2021 showed that selecting /which/ LNS variant
to call at /which/ node can itself be learned. SCIP's =heur_alns.c=
implements a classical multi-armed-bandit controller over RENS, RINS, DINS,
mutation, crossover, proximity, zeroobj, and trust region, updating arm
weights based on observed solution improvements. The successor
=heur_scheduler.c= (SCIP 8+, replacing ALNS by default in SCIP 9) integrates
a learned policy that schedules both LNS arms and diving arms in a single
framework. The controller itself is pure Python-level logic — a weight
update and an arm choice per call. For discopt, implementing ALNS requires
only (1) a common interface for LNS arms (each a Python function
=(model, incumbent, budget) -> Optional[Solution]=) and (2) a bandit
controller that updates arm weights from solve outcomes.

** Port-Order Recommendation

Given discopt's stack, the practical port order within the LNS family is:

1. **RENS and RINS first** — highest benchmark impact, simplest API
   requirements (=Model.clone()= + =Model.fix_variable()=).
2. **Local branching** — adds the distance-constraint API that DINS and
   proximity search depend on.
3. **DINS and proximity search** — build on the same API.
4. **Crossover and mutation** — require an incumbent pool, which discopt's
   =SolveResult= already tracks.
5. **GINS** — requires the variable-constraint bipartite graph, which
   discopt's expression DAG already exposes (see
   [[file:../concepts/expression-dag-and-ad.org][expression DAG and AD]]).
6. **ALNS / Scheduler controller** — a thin coordinator over the above.

Every step is Python orchestration; none requires Rust changes beyond the
clone/restrict/recurse API surface that the first port establishes.

** Role in discopt

LNS heuristics are the primary mechanism by which an early incumbent is
turned into an /optimal/ incumbent. Once discopt's B&B tree is producing
feasible solutions from the feasibility pump (see
[[file:feasibility-pump.org][feasibility pump]]) and from rounding / shifting heuristics (see
[[file:mip-primal-heuristics.org][MIP primal heuristics]]), LNS takes over to polish the incumbent by local
refinement. The compounding effect on primal-dual gap closure is large: on
structured MIPLIB instances, the difference between "has LNS" and "no LNS"
is often a factor of 2-5 in final gap after a fixed time limit, even when
the LP bound and branching strategy are held constant.
