Neither [[file:../concepts/scip-solver.org][SCIP]] nor [[file:../concepts/highs-solver.org][HiGHS]] ships learning-based primal heuristics as a first-class
part of their portfolios — both rely on the hand-tuned, structure-specific
heuristics catalogued in [[file:mip-primal-heuristics.org][MIP primal heuristics]] and
[[file:large-neighborhood-search.org][large neighborhood search]]. Yet the post-2020 literature has produced a
coherent family of /learned/ heuristics that share a common
graph-neural-network (GNN) backbone, exploit large MIP datasets (MIPLIB +
application corpora), and consistently outperform hand-tuned rules on the
distributions they are trained on. For discopt, these heuristics are an
architectural fit: discopt already has a Python modeling layer, a JAX backend
for numerics, and an LLM/ML subsystem in =python/discopt/llm/= that can host
GNN models, dataset management, and inference. This article surveys the
family and records the port plan.

** The Shared GNN Backbone: Bipartite Variable-Constraint Encoder

Every modern learning-based MIP heuristic is built on the bipartite
variable-constraint graph representation introduced by
citet:gasse-2019 for learning-to-branch and subsequently reused for diving,
LNS destroy selection, and predict-and-search. The graph has one node per
variable and one per constraint; each variable-constraint edge carries the
coefficient value as a feature. A graph convolutional network passes
messages along edges, producing a per-variable embedding that summarizes
local structure, coefficient magnitudes, and objective alignment.

This representation is agnostic to problem size and structure, which makes
it the right substrate for transfer learning across problem classes. For
discopt the important implication is that the /same/ GNN encoder serves
Neural Diving, Neural LNS, and Predict-and-Search; the heuristic-specific
machinery lives in task heads on top of the shared encoder. A single
pre-training run on MIPLIB2017 + MINLPLib + discopt's own benchmark traces
amortizes the cost across the full learning-based family. **Host: JAX**
(encoder and heads); **Python** (dataset generation, training loop).

** Neural Diving (Nair et al. 2020)

citet:nair-neural-diving-2020 — a DeepMind paper that introduced both
Neural Diving and Neural Branching — trains a GNN to predict, for each
integer variable, the probability that it takes each of a small set of
candidate values in an optimal (or near-optimal) solution. At solve time,
the GNN is evaluated once on the root-node problem. Variables with high-
confidence predictions are /fixed/ to the predicted values; the residual
MIP is solved as a small sub-MIP. The structure is identical to RENS
citep:berthold-rens-2014 (see [[file:large-neighborhood-search.org][LNS]]) except that the fixing rule is
learned rather than derived from LP integrality.

The paper reports substantial wall-clock improvements on large MIPs —
specifically on instances where the LP relaxation is expensive and where
RENS's "fix the already-integer LP values" rule is uninformative. Training
data is generated by running an external MIP solver (SCIP or Gurobi) to
optimality on a corpus of instances; the resulting (instance, optimal
assignment) pairs supervise the GNN.

**Host: Python driver + JAX GNN.** The orchestration (model.clone, fix
variables, recursive solve) is identical to RENS and reuses the same
discopt API. The JAX GNN does one forward pass per call; inference is
milliseconds on a GPU and tens of milliseconds on a CPU.

** Neural Branching

citet:nair-neural-diving-2020 also introduced Neural Branching: a second
task head that predicts strong-branching scores directly from the GNN
embedding, replacing the full-strong-branching oracle whose cost dominates
modern branching strategies. Reliability pseudocost branching already
mitigates this cost on CPU; Neural Branching offers an alternative that is
GPU-parallel and scales to very deep search trees. **Host: Python driver +
JAX GNN.** For discopt, Neural Branching is a natural companion to the
learned heuristic stack but lives in the branching module rather than the
heuristic module; it is noted here for completeness.

** Learning to Destroy: Neural LNS

citet:sonnerat-neural-lns-2021 and citet:wu-neural-lns-2021 (NeurIPS 2021)
independently proposed using a GNN to decide, at each LNS iteration, which
subset of variables to /destroy/ (unfix) and which to keep fixed. The
choice of destroy set is the step that most strongly determines LNS
performance — RINS fixes LP/incumbent agreements, Local Branching uses a
Hamming ball, DINS combines both, but none of these rules adapts to
instance structure. The learned destroy operator, trained by reinforcement
learning on sub-MIP outcomes, selects destroy sets that lead to faster
improvements on the training distribution.

Neural LNS slots into the ALNS-style controller discussed in
[[file:large-neighborhood-search.org][LNS]] as an additional arm: the bandit coordinator picks among RINS, RENS,
DINS, crossover, proximity, and Neural LNS, with the arm weights learned
from observed improvements. **Host: Python driver + JAX GNN.** Each LNS
call costs one GNN inference plus one sub-MIP solve; the overhead relative
to RINS is the GNN inference, which is negligible for sub-MIPs of
non-trivial size.

** Predict-and-Search (Han et al. ICLR 2023)

citet:han-predict-search-2023 combine Neural Diving's prediction step with
a /trust-region/ search: the GNN predicts a full candidate assignment
$\hat{x}$, and the solver is then asked to find a solution within a
Hamming ball of radius $k$ around $\hat{x}$. The trust-region formulation
is a single linear constraint (identical in form to Local Branching
citep:fischetti-lodi-2003) and the sub-MIP is typically much easier than
the original problem. Predict-and-Search is state of the art on several
MIPLIB hard-instance families at the time of writing and is the strongest
single-shot learning-based heuristic. **Host: Python driver + JAX GNN.**

** Contrastive and Self-Supervised Pretraining

citet:huang-mipgnn-2023 proposed contrastive pretraining for the GNN
encoder: two views of the same MIP (e.g., scaled, permuted, or slightly
perturbed) should yield similar embeddings, and two views of different
MIPs should not. This mirrors the SimCLR/MoCo recipe from computer vision.
The resulting pretrained encoder transfers across heuristic tasks — the
same backbone is used for learning-to-branch, learning-to-destroy, and
learning-to-dive without task-specific retraining.

For discopt this is the /foundation model/ argument: a single encoder
trained once on a large heterogeneous MIP corpus, reused across every
learning-based heuristic. The alternative — per-task supervised training
— requires a fresh labeled dataset per heuristic and does not amortize
across tasks. **Host: Python + JAX.**

** Learned Scheduler of Heuristics

citet:chmiela-learn-schedule-2021 (NeurIPS 2021) takes a different tack:
rather than learning /what/ each heuristic should do, learn /when/ each
heuristic should run. The scheduler observes instance features and
historical improvements, and picks which heuristic (diving, LNS, pump,
rounding) to invoke at each B&B node. SCIP 9's =heur_scheduler.c=
citep:bolusani-scip9-2024 implements this idea in C; the learned policy
itself is small and the training data are traces of SCIP solves. For
discopt, the scheduler is a thin Python layer over the full heuristic
portfolio and is orthogonal to the content of the arms themselves. It
should be implemented once the Rust + Python + JAX heuristic stack has
enough arms to make scheduling non-trivial (medium-term, see roadmap).
**Host: Python.**

** Dataset Generation

Learning-based heuristics require training data. The standard pattern is:

1. Collect a diverse MIP corpus (MIPLIB2017 + MINLPLib + discopt-native
   benchmark instances).
2. Run a reference solver (SCIP, Gurobi, or discopt itself once it is
   competitive) to optimality or near-optimality on each instance.
3. Record per-node features, per-variable labels (optimal values), and
   per-heuristic outcomes (which heuristic produced the best incumbent at
   each node).

For discopt this pipeline should live in =discopt_benchmarks/= alongside
the existing correctness harness. Pre-training runs once per architecture
revision; fine-tuning is cheap and per-task.

** License and Reproducibility

All the cited papers ship reference implementations in TensorFlow, PyTorch,
or JAX under permissive licenses (MIT or Apache-2.0). The algorithms
themselves are paper-described and not copyrightable in the US
(17 USC §102(b)); clean-room reimplementation in JAX is the norm.
discopt's own implementation should cite each paper, preserve any upstream
attributions in per-file headers when code is adapted, and make the
pretrained weights downloadable alongside each release.

** Role in discopt

Learning-based heuristics are discopt's strongest architectural fit
outside of differentiable LP (see [[file:first-order-mip-heuristics.org][first-order MIP heuristics]]). The
pieces — JAX numerics, Python orchestration, bipartite graph
representation, LLM/ML subsystem — already exist. The missing components
are (1) the shared GNN encoder, (2) the task heads, and (3) a
training/dataset pipeline. Each is paper-described and implementable in
~500 lines of JAX per task. The medium-term plan is to build the bipartite
graph representation in Rust with a JAX-friendly Python view, train a
shared encoder on MIPLIB, and ship Neural Diving + Neural LNS as the first
two task heads. Predict-and-Search and the learned scheduler follow once
the encoder is stable.

This article is paired with [[file:first-order-mip-heuristics.org][first-order MIP heuristics]] (which covers the
GPU/differentiable-LP angle) and [[file:matheuristics.org][matheuristics]] (which covers
structure-aware classical heuristics). Together they cover what SCIP and
HiGHS omit, and define where discopt's post-SCIP differentiation lives.
