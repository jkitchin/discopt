Interactive multiobjective optimization alternates between /computation/
and /preference elicitation/. The DM neither specifies full preferences
up front nor waits for a complete Pareto-front approximation; instead
they inspect one (or a few) Pareto-optimal candidate(s), supply local
preference feedback, and the algorithm generates new candidates in the
region of interest. Interactive methods are the practical method of
choice when (i) the Pareto front is too large to enumerate, (ii) the DM
cannot articulate full trade-off preferences a priori, or (iii) each
subproblem solve is expensive citep:miettinen-1999.

** Reference-Point Methodology

citet:wierzbicki-2000 is the foundational framework. The DM supplies a
/reference point/ z^ref (a desired aspiration level for each objective).
The algorithm computes the Pareto-optimal point closest to z^ref in the
achievement-function sense:

  min  s(F; z^ref, w, rho)
      = max_i w_i (f_i(x) - z^ref_i) + rho sum_i w_i (f_i(x) - z^ref_i).

The DM inspects the result, adjusts z^ref (move it in the direction of
desired improvement), and re-solves. This iterative loop converges to a
Pareto-optimal point the DM accepts, without requiring numeric weights.

Key reference-point methods:

- /Reference point method/ (Wierzbicki): the basic loop above.
- /GUESS/: DM sees one Pareto point and specifies objectives to improve
  or relax.
- /Light beam search/: DM specifies a reservation point and an
  aspiration point; outputs a small sample of Pareto points between
  them.
- /Reference direction/ and /Pareto race/: the DM navigates along a
  direction in objective space, generating a sequence of Pareto points
  in real time.

See citep:miettinen-1999 for a comprehensive classification.

** NIMBUS

citet:miettinen-2008-pse (see also Miettinen & Mäkelä 1995) introduced
NIMBUS, the most widely-used interactive method in chemical-process and
engineering-design applications. At each iteration the DM sees one
Pareto-optimal candidate F(x) and classifies each objective into one of
five categories:

1. f_i should /improve/ as much as possible.
2. f_i should /improve/ at least to a specified aspiration level z_i.
3. f_i is /satisfactory/ and should stay put.
4. f_i may /worsen/ up to an upper bound z_i.
5. f_i may change /freely/.

NIMBUS then builds and solves up to four scalarized subproblems that
realize this classification (a reference-point formulation, a weighted
Tchebycheff, a GUESS-like formulation, and an achievement function), and
presents the resulting Pareto-optimal candidates. The DM picks one, and
the loop continues. This richer single-iteration output helps DMs
understand local trade-off geometry without having to enumerate the
front.

NIMBUS was formulated originally for nondifferentiable problems and is
widely applied in chemical engineering --- paper-machine design,
bioprocess optimization, catalyst synthesis trade-offs --- where
objectives are evaluated by simulation and the number of affordable
solves per iteration is small citep:miettinen-2008-pse.

** Interactive Evolutionary Methods

Recent work couples reference-point methodology with EMO algorithms:

- /R-NSGA-II/ focuses the nondominated front generation around
  DM-supplied reference points, giving a dense local approximation
  rather than a uniform global one.
- /Preference-based MOEA/D/ distorts the weight-vector distribution
  toward the DM's region of interest.
- /Progressive articulation of preferences/: periodic preference queries
  during an EMO run that reshape selection pressure.

These hybrids aim to retain the single-run nature of EMO while
delivering a front concentrated where the DM cares, rather than the
uninformative uniform coverage of classic NSGA-II.

** When to Use Interactive Methods

| Situation                                           | Method class         |
|-----------------------------------------------------+----------------------|
| Cheap subproblems, k = 2, DM wants to see the front | A posteriori scalarization |
| Expensive subproblems, DM has rough aspirations     | Reference point / NIMBUS   |
| Many objectives, DM cannot rank                     | Interactive EMO (R-NSGA, MOEA/D-pref) |
| Black-box, expensive, low-budget                    | Interactive Bayesian (preference-EHVI) |

Interactive methods are the most "human-in-the-loop" branch of
multiobjective optimization and typically require GUI tooling (IND-NIMBUS,
DESDEO) more than algorithmic sophistication.

** Relevance for discopt

Any interactive method reduces to repeated scalar subproblems: a
reference-point or achievement subproblem, a weighted Tchebycheff, or an
ε-constraint formulation. discopt's single-objective API can serve any
of these provided the scalarized subproblem is expressible as a standard
MINLP. A future discopt multi-objective package would naturally layer an
interactive frontend (e.g., a Jupyter / widget-based NIMBUS or
reference-point loop) on top of the ~ParetoFront~ scalarization engine.
