Multiobjective evolutionary algorithms (MOEAs) evolve a /population/ of
candidate solutions toward the Pareto front in a single run, approximating
the entire front rather than one point at a time. They are the dominant
class for black-box MOPs, problems where gradients are unavailable,
problems with discrete variables handled by genetic operators, and for
benchmarking against exact scalarization methods. The field is broadly
divided into /dominance-based/, /decomposition-based/, and
/indicator-based/ lineages citep:emmerich-deutz-2018.

** Dominance-Based MOEAs

*** NSGA-II

citet:deb-2002 defined the reference dominance-based MOEA. Each generation:

1. /Fast nondominated sorting/ ranks the combined parent+offspring
   population into Pareto fronts F_1, F_2, ..., F_k in O(M N^2) time
   (M objectives, N individuals).
2. /Crowding distance/ within each front measures the L1 spacing to
   immediate neighbors in objective space, preferring individuals in
   less-populated regions.
3. Elitist replacement: fill the next population by filling fronts in
   order, breaking ties in the last admitted front by crowding distance.

NSGA-II's simplicity, O(M N^2) per generation cost, and constraint
handling via tournament selection on a constrained-dominance relation
made it the default MOEA for k = 2, 3 objectives for over a decade
citep:deb-2002. Its signature failure mode is /loss of selection
pressure at many objectives/: once the population is all mutually
nondominated (common for k >= 4), crowding distance is the only signal
and convergence stalls.

*** SPEA2 and Indicator-Based Variants

citet:zitzler-thiele-1999 introduced SPEA (Strength Pareto EA), which
assigns a /strength/ to each individual (number of others it dominates)
and fitness equal to the sum of strengths of dominators. SPEA2 (Zitzler
et al. 2001, commonly cited from the TIK technical report) added density
estimation by k-th nearest neighbor in objective space. SPEA2 is more
robust on nonconvex fronts than NSGA-II but computationally heavier.

/Indicator-based EA/ (IBEA) citep:zitzler-kunzli-2004 generalizes this:
pick any dominance-compliant indicator I (e.g., hypervolume loss or
binary epsilon), and define fitness as the summed indicator contribution
of each individual. IBEA gives a principled path to many-objective
optimization --- the indicator itself encodes what "better" means when
pure dominance is not discriminative --- and motivates the
hypervolume-based SMS-EMOA and HypE algorithms. See
[[file:../concepts/performance-indicators-mo.org][performance indicators]] for indicator definitions.

*** PAES

citet:knowles-corne-2000 proposed PAES (Pareto Archived Evolution
Strategy), a minimalist (1+1)-ES that maintains an archive of
nondominated solutions and accepts offspring via a grid-based crowding
heuristic. PAES is mostly of historical interest but its /external
archive/ idea underlies nearly every modern MOEA and Bayesian
multi-objective method.

** Decomposition-Based MOEAs

*** MOEA/D

citet:zhang-li-2007 decomposes the MOP into N scalar subproblems using a
chosen [[file:scalarization-methods.org][scalarization]] (typically weighted Tchebycheff or penalty-based
boundary intersection) with N direction vectors that cover the objective
space. Each individual in the population owns one subproblem and its
neighborhood (the B nearest directions). Genetic operators combine
solutions only with neighbors; each offspring is evaluated on all
neighborhood subproblems and replaces the incumbent where it improves
the scalarized objective.

Key properties citep:zhang-li-2007 citep:trivedi-2017:

- Constant per-individual cost O(B) rather than O(N^2) dominance sort;
  scales to large populations.
- Explicit parameter control: the weight vectors determine coverage,
  which can be uniform (simplex-lattice) or tailored to a DM.
- Naturally many-objective: decomposition sidesteps the dominance
  degeneracy of NSGA-II.
- Sensitive to weight-vector / population-size coupling (structured
  lattices required for k >= 3) and to reference-point estimation.

MOEA/D spawned a large family (MOEA/D-DE, MOEA/D-DRA, MOEA/D-M2M);
citet:trivedi-2017 is the canonical survey.

*** NSGA-III

citet:deb-jain-2014 combined the dominance structure of NSGA-II with
decomposition-style /reference points/ for many-objective problems. The
algorithm keeps NSGA-II's nondominated-sorting selection but, within
the last admitted front, selects individuals to preserve association
with a set of user-supplied or simplex-generated reference directions.
NSGA-III is the current reference algorithm for 3--15 objectives in the
pymoo / PlatEMO ecosystem citep:blank-deb-2020 citep:tian-2017.

** Indicator-Based MOEAs

Hypervolume-driven selection (SMS-EMOA, HypE) computes each individual's
/hypervolume contribution/ and removes the least-contributing individual
in environmental selection. In the limit of infinite population, the
optimal distribution is the one that maximizes hypervolume on the true
front citep:zitzler-kunzli-2004. Cost scales with hypervolume
computation (exponential in k); approximations (HypE's Monte-Carlo
hypervolume) extend these methods to many-objective problems.

** Constraint Handling

Three dominant approaches citep:deb-2002 citep:emmerich-deutz-2018:

1. /Constrained dominance/ (Deb): a feasible point dominates any
   infeasible one; among infeasibles, the one with smaller constraint
   violation dominates. Simple and fits dominance-based MOEAs.
2. /Penalty functions/: add a weighted sum of violations to each
   objective. Sensitive to penalty tuning.
3. /Repair operators/: project infeasible solutions onto the feasible
   region. Problem-specific.

For MINLP-style constraints, none of these is competitive with
scalarization + a real MINLP solver in terms of solution quality or
certificate strength; MOEAs are the tool of choice when the problem is
black-box or when only a fast Pareto-front approximation is required.

** Software Ecosystem

- *pymoo* citep:blank-deb-2020: Python library with NSGA-II/III, MOEA/D,
  R-NSGA, AGE-MOEA, indicators, test problems, and decomposition helpers.
  Standard choice for Python workflows.
- *PlatEMO* citep:tian-2017: MATLAB platform with >100 MOEAs, >50 test
  problems, and a GUI. Standard for algorithm benchmarking in the EMO
  community.
- *DEAP*, *Platypus*, *inspyred*: general evolutionary frameworks with
  MOEA support, lower coupling and more flexibility than pymoo but
  fewer canned MOEAs.

** When to Use MOEAs with discopt

Evolutionary methods and exact MINLP are complementary rather than
competing:

- Use MOEAs directly when objectives or constraints are black-box,
  expensive to evaluate, or nonsmooth.
- Use MOEAs as a /warm start/ provider: pymoo's NSGA-II output gives
  initial points and range estimates (z*, z^N) for an exact
  scalarization sweep using discopt.
- Use discopt as the /subproblem solver/ inside a decomposition-style
  framework: each MOEA/D scalar subproblem is a standard
  [[file:../concepts/minlp-survey.org][MINLP]] and inherits discopt's optimality certificates.
